\begin{abstract}
Autonomous driving (AD) is one of the most demanding challenges in modern
artificial intelligence, requiring real-time perception, intent prediction of
heterogeneous road users, and safe, socially compliant decision-making in a
continuously evolving multi-agent environment.
This survey provides a systematic and comprehensive review of the field,
organized around a three-layer taxonomy that traces the evolution from
single-agent foundations to fully cognitive driving systems.

\textbf{Layer 1---Foundational Single-Agent RL.}
We begin by formalizing the AD decision problem as a Partially Observable
Markov Decision Process (POMDP)~\cite{hubmann2018automated} and surveying the
algorithmic building blocks that underpin all subsequent work: end-to-end
behavior cloning~\cite{bojarski2016end}, augmentation strategies to overcome
covariate shift~\cite{bansal2018chauffeur}, conditional imitation
learning~\cite{codevilla2018end}, and policy gradient optimization including
PPO~\cite{schulman2017proximal} and its deep RL derivatives~\cite{sallab2017deep,kiran2021deep}.

\textbf{Layer 2---MARL Algorithms and Coordination Protocols.}
We then extend the formalism to the Partially Observable Stochastic Game (POSG)
framework~\cite{littman1994markov,bernstein2002complexity}, where the
non-stationarity and exponential joint-action space of traffic demand specialized
multi-agent methods.  We survey value-decomposition approaches
(VDN~\cite{sunehag2018value}, QMIX~\cite{rashid2018qmix}, QTRAN~\cite{son2019qtran}),
actor-critic CTDE methods (MADDPG~\cite{lowe2017multi},
COMA~\cite{foerster2018counterfactual}, MAPPO~\cite{yu2021surprising},
HATRPO~\cite{kuba2021hatrpo}), role-based architectures
(RODE~\cite{wang2021rode}, ROMA~\cite{wang2020roma}), hierarchical
MARL~\cite{vezhnevets2017feudal,chu2020multi}, and game-theoretic
approaches~\cite{shapley1953stochastic,littman1994markov,hu2003nash,foerster2018lola}.
For communication and coordination, we trace the progression from early
learned protocols~\cite{foerster2016learning,sukhbaatar2016learning} to
targeted attention-based messaging~\cite{das2019tarmac,jiang2018learning},
graph-structured communication~\cite{jiang2019graph,kim2019learning}, and
V2X cooperative perception~\cite{wang2020v2vnet}.
On the representation side, we analyze the evolution from grid social
pooling~\cite{alahi2016social} to vectorized GNNs~\cite{gao2020vectornet,liang2020lanegcn},
multi-modal prediction~\cite{gupta2018social,salzmann2020trajectron,zhao2020tnt},
and bidirectional prediction--planning coupling~\cite{song2020pip}.

\textbf{Layer 3---Scalable and Transformer/LLM-Augmented Agents.}
The final layer surveys the nascent paradigm of cognitive driving agents,
including Multi-Agent Transformer architectures~\cite{wen2022multi},
Decision Transformers~\cite{chen2021decision}, and LLM-based systems
that inject common-sense reasoning and few-shot
generalization~\cite{fu2023drive,wen2023dilu,mao2023gpt,sha2023languagempc}.
We also survey critical simulation environments (CARLA~\cite{dosovitskiy2017carlaopenurbandriving},
SMARTS~\cite{zhou2020smarts}), benchmarks
(NuPlan~\cite{caesar2021nuplan}, Argoverse~\cite{chang2019argoverse},
Waymo~\cite{ettinger2021large}), and safety frameworks~\cite{shalev2016safe,achiam2017constrained}.

Across all paradigms, we synthesize the key technical contributions,
identify the precise limitations each work addresses, and draw cross-cutting
connections.  The survey covers 100 primary references spanning 2003 to 2024,
and concludes with a structured analysis of open challenges: formal safety
verification under distributional shift, bandwidth-constrained V2X communication,
real-time LLM inference, and the need for generative world models to cover
long-tail corner cases.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Autonomous Driving, Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning,
Centralized Training Decentralized Execution, Trajectory Prediction, Graph Neural
Networks, Vehicle-to-Vehicle Communication, Large Language Models, Cognitive Agents,
Safety, Simulation.

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The pursuit of fully autonomous driving (AD) represents one of the most
transformative yet technically demanding frontiers in modern artificial
intelligence.  It requires agents to simultaneously perceive complex,
dynamic environments with high fidelity; predict the intentions of
heterogeneous road users ranging from aggressive merging vehicles to
unpredictable pedestrians; and execute safe, efficient, and socially
compliant maneuvers under continuous time pressure.  Critically, these
tasks are not independent: the optimal action for the ego-vehicle depends
on the predicted behavior of surrounding agents, which in turn depends on
the ego-vehicle's own future actions.  This mutual dependence renders
autonomous driving a canonical \emph{multi-agent} decision problem in
which the performance of any single policy is fundamentally coupled to the
policies of all other actors in the scene.

Despite decades of engineering effort, achieving the level of robustness and
generalization required for unrestricted public deployment on diverse road
types and in novel scenarios remains an open research problem.  Failures
continue to occur precisely in situations that are statistically rare (so-called
\emph{long-tail} events) yet critically important for safety: unusual road
geometries, ambiguous right-of-way negotiations, adversarial pedestrian
behavior, or unexpected sensor degradation.  The historical arc of the field
can be understood as a sequence of three successive paradigm shifts, each
motivated by well-defined limitations of its predecessor.

% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Motivation and Problem Statement}
\label{sec:motivation}

Classical autonomous driving systems relied on a modular pipeline
architecture that decomposes the complex driving task into distinct,
hierarchical sub-systems: perception, localization, prediction, planning,
and control~\cite{caesar2021nuplan}.  While this ``divide-and-conquer''
approach offers engineering interpretability and allows individual modules
to be validated in isolation, it suffers from systematic limitations.
Most importantly, it is prone to \emph{cascading errors}: a minor
imperfection in the perception module (e.g., a flickering bounding box at
the boundary of sensor range) can propagate downstream, destabilizing the
planner and leading to conservative, suboptimal, or even dangerous
behavior.  Furthermore, hand-crafted rules in the planning module fail to
capture the nuanced, interactive nature of human driving, resulting in
behavior that is either overly conservative or strategically naive.

The promise of data-driven, end-to-end learning as an alternative was first
demonstrated at scale by \citeauthor{bojarski2016end}~\cite{bojarski2016end},
who showed that a convolutional neural network mapping raw front-camera images
directly to steering commands could navigate real roads without explicit
intermediate representations.  However, the limitations of pure imitation
learning quickly became apparent: the \emph{covariate shift} problem, wherein
the agent drifts into states absent from the training distribution and has no
learned recovery mechanism, remained a fundamental obstacle~\cite{bansal2018chauffeur}.
More deeply, pure imitation cannot model the \emph{strategic interactions}
between agents: the response of a merging vehicle depends not only on its own
preferences but also on its prediction of the ego-vehicle's intent, creating a
feedback loop that single-agent behavior cloning cannot represent.

This interactive structure formally defines autonomous driving as a
\emph{multi-agent} problem.  In a traffic environment with $N$ vehicles,
each agent $i$ observes a partial view $o^i_t$ of the world state $s_t$,
executes an action $a^i_t$ from its action space $\mathcal{A}^i$, and
receives a reward $R^i(s_t, \mathbf{a}_t)$ that depends on the
\emph{joint} action vector $\mathbf{a}_t = (a^1_t, \ldots, a^N_t)$.
The transition dynamics
$T(s_{t+1} \mid s_t, \mathbf{a}_t)$ couple all agents together, so
no single agent can treat the environment as stationary while other
agents are simultaneously adapting their
policies~\cite{zhang2021multi,busoniu2008comprehensive}.

This non-stationarity violates the foundational Markov assumption required
for convergence of standard single-agent RL algorithms.  \citeauthor{shalev2016safe}~\cite{shalev2016safe}
further show that even if non-stationarity is managed, the gradient
variance of policy gradient methods scales as $O(1/p^2)$ for accident
probability $p \ll 1$, making unconstrained RL impractically slow for
safety-critical applications.  The central research question motivating
this survey is therefore:

\begin{quote}
\emph{How have MARL algorithms, coordination protocols, and cognitive
architectures evolved to address the cooperative and competitive challenges
inherent in multi-agent traffic environments, and what are the remaining
open problems?}
\end{quote}

\subsection{Driving Forces Behind Paradigm Shifts}
\label{sec:drivers}

The field has not evolved uniformly; rather, it has undergone discrete
paradigm shifts each driven by a convergence of factors.  We identify four
primary categories of driving forces:

\subsubsection*{(1) Algorithmic Breakthroughs}
The development of deep Q-networks (DQN) by \citeauthor{mnih2015human}~\cite{mnih2015human}
demonstrated that deep neural networks could learn superhuman strategies for
Atari games directly from raw pixels, immediately suggesting that similar
methods could be applied to driving perception and control.  Policy gradient
methods (REINFORCE, A3C/A2C)~\cite{mnih2016asynchronous} and their proximal
trust-region variants (TRPO, PPO~\cite{schulman2017proximal}) provided stable
and scalable optimization for continuous-action driving tasks.  The discovery
of the CTDE (Centralized Training, Decentralized Execution) framework by
\citeauthor{kraemer2016multi}~\cite{kraemer2016multi} and its instantiations
in MADDPG~\cite{lowe2017multi} and QMIX~\cite{rashid2018qmix} unlocked
principled cooperative MARL.  Graph neural networks
(GAT~\cite{velickovic2018graph}, GCN-based policy networks~\cite{jiang2019graph})
provided the relational inductive biases needed to encode agent-to-agent
and agent-to-map relationships compactly.  Most recently, Transformer
architectures~\cite{vaswani2017attention} and large language
models~\cite{brown2020language} have begun reshaping the cognitive layer of
driving agents~\cite{wen2022multi,fu2023drive,sha2023languagempc}.

\subsubsection*{(2) Hardware and Compute Scaling}
The availability of large GPU clusters has made it practical to train policies
on billions of environment steps, a prerequisite for sample-hungry MARL
algorithms.  Real-world sensor packages---combining high-resolution LiDAR,
multi-camera arrays, GPS/IMU, and radar---have reached cost levels that enable
fleet-scale data collection, providing the large-scale supervision necessary for
pre-training foundation models for driving~\cite{caesar2020nuscenes}.

\subsubsection*{(3) Dataset and Simulation Ecosystem Maturation}
High-fidelity, open-source driving simulators (CARLA~\cite{dosovitskiy2017carlaopenurbandriving},
SUMO~\cite{krajzewicz2012recent}) and domain-specific MARL training
platforms (SMARTS~\cite{zhou2020smarts}, MACAD-Gym~\cite{palanisamy2020multi},
Flow~\cite{wu2017flow}) have dramatically reduced the barrier to multi-agent
experimentation.  Simultaneously, large-scale real-world driving datasets
(nuScenes~\cite{caesar2020nuscenes}, Argoverse~\cite{chang2019argoverse},
Waymo Open Motion~\cite{ettinger2021large}) and closed-loop planning
benchmarks (NuPlan~\cite{caesar2021nuplan}) have standardized evaluation
and exposed the limitations of open-loop metrics.

\subsubsection*{(4) Real-World Deployment Demands}
Commercial robotaxi programs (Waymo, Cruise, Baidu Apollo) have pushed
research toward long-tail robustness, edge-case handling, and
interpretability---precisely the domains where purely data-driven methods
struggle.  Emerging V2X infrastructure standards (C-V2X, DSRC) have
created new opportunities for cooperative perception and planning that
transcend what any single-vehicle sensor suite can
achieve~\cite{wang2020v2vnet}.

\subsubsection*{Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria}
\textbf{Included}: peer-reviewed conference and journal papers, and
preprints with $\geq 50$ citations that directly address either (a) MARL
methodology applicable to driving, or (b) AD planning/prediction/control
with a learning component.
\textbf{Excluded}: purely perception-only methods without a planning
component, rule-based planners without any learning, and non-vehicular
multi-robot work without demonstrated relevance to road scenarios.
The final corpus comprises \textbf{100 primary references}.

\subsection{Three-Layer Taxonomy}
\label{sec:taxonomy}

The organizing framework of this survey consists of three hierarchical
layers that map to successive paradigm shifts in the field:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, label=\textbf{Layer \arabic*.}]
  \item \textbf{Algorithmic Foundations (Paradigm I, $\approx$2003--2018).}
        This layer covers the theoretical formalisms (MDP, POMDP, Stochastic
        Games, Dec-POMDP), the deep RL building blocks (DQN, A3C, PPO,
        DDPG, SAC), and the early single-agent driving methods (DAVE-2,
        ChauffeurNet, conditional imitation learning).  The central
        limitation exposed here is that single-agent learning is
        fundamentally insufficient for dense, interactive traffic because
        it cannot account for the non-stationarity induced by other
        learning agents.

  \item \textbf{Coordination and Communication (Paradigm II, $\approx$2017--2022).}
        This layer addresses the core MARL challenge through three parallel
        thrusts: (a) \emph{MARL algorithms} that achieve stable cooperative
        learning (value decomposition, CTDE actor-critic, hierarchical and
        game-theoretic methods); (b) \emph{communication protocols} that
        enable agents to share information selectively and efficiently
        under bandwidth constraints; and (c) \emph{relational representation
        and prediction--planning integration} using GNNs and vectorized
        scene encoders.  The central limitation remaining at the end of
        this paradigm is brittleness in long-tail scenarios that fall
        outside the training distribution.

  \item \textbf{Scalable and Cognitive Architectures (Paradigm III, $\approx$2021--present).}
        This layer covers Transformer-based MARL architectures that scale
        attention over large agent populations, and LLM-driven cognitive
        agents that inject world-scale knowledge and chain-of-thought
        reasoning to handle rare, ambiguous scenarios.  Safety, sim-to-real
        transfer, and world-model-based planning are cross-cutting concerns
        in this layer.
\end{enumerate}

Critically, the layers are not mutually exclusive: modern systems
routinely combine elements from all three (e.g., a GNN scene encoder
from Layer~2 coupled with an LLM reasoning module from Layer~3, trained
via MAPPO from Layer~2, within a CARLA environment from Layer~2).  The
taxonomy is therefore better understood as a set of conceptual lenses
rather than a strict chronological partition.

\subsection{Comparison with Existing Surveys and Contributions}
\label{sec:comparison}

A number of surveys have addressed adjacent topics, but none covers the
full scope of this work:

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{General MARL surveys}~\cite{busoniu2008comprehensive,zhang2021multi}:
        provide excellent algorithmic coverage but lack AD-specific
        application depth, MARL platform comparisons, and the emerging
        LLM/cognitive agent dimension.
  \item \textbf{AD-RL surveys}~\cite{kiran2021deep,sallab2017deep}:
        primarily address single-agent methods; MARL is treated at
        most as an advanced topic in a few paragraphs, with no systematic
        comparison of CTDE methods, communication protocols, or
        value-decomposition approaches.
  \item \textbf{Cooperative driving reviews}: typically narrow in scope,
        covering either traffic-signal control or V2X cooperative
        perception in isolation, without connecting these to the broader
        MARL literature.
  \item \textbf{Trajectory prediction surveys}: excellent coverage of
        motion forecasting methods but generally absent of any connection
        to the policy-learning and multi-agent coordination literature.
\end{itemize}

The unique contributions of this survey are fourfold:
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Unified three-layer taxonomy} that integrates algorithmic,
        representational, and cognitive perspectives under a single
        organizational framework.
  \item \textbf{Systematic comparison of 100 papers}: for each method
        we explicitly state the limitation it addresses, the technical
        mechanism it introduces, and the residual limitations that
        motivated subsequent work.
  \item \textbf{Critical limitation analysis per category}: rather than
        cataloguing results, we trace causal chains between methods,
        making the survey a narrative of scientific progress rather than
        an annotated bibliography.
  \item \textbf{Structured open-challenges mapping}: we distinguish
        between short-term engineering challenges (computational overhead
        of LLM inference, standardizing V2X feature representations)
        and long-term research challenges (formal safety certificates for
        neuro-symbolic systems, generative world models for distributional
        coverage).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background and Formal Foundations}
\label{sec:background}

This section establishes the mathematical and conceptual foundations upon which
the remainder of the survey is built.  We begin with the single-agent
formalism---Markov Decision Processes (MDPs) and Partially Observable Markov
Decision Processes (POMDPs)---before extending the framework to accommodate
multiple interacting agents through stochastic games and Decentralized POMDPs.
We then briefly review the core deep reinforcement learning algorithms that
underpin the methods discussed in later sections, introduce graph neural networks
as a tool for relational reasoning over traffic scenes, and situate the learning
components within the modular autonomous driving software stack.

% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Single-Agent Formalism: MDP and POMDP}
\label{sec:mdp_pomdp}

\subsubsection*{Markov Decision Process}

A \emph{Markov Decision Process} (MDP) provides the canonical mathematical
framework for sequential decision-making under uncertainty.  Formally, an MDP
is defined by the tuple
$\mathcal{M} = \langle \mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, P, R, \gamma \rangle$,
where:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item $\mathcal{S}$ is the \textbf{state space}, representing all possible
        configurations of the world (e.g., positions, velocities, and headings
        of all vehicles on the road);
  \item $\mathcal{A}$ is the \textbf{action space}, representing the decisions
        available to the agent (e.g., discrete maneuvers such as accelerate,
        brake, or steer, or continuous values for throttle, steering angle, and
        braking);
  \item $P: \mathcal{S} \times \mathcal{A} \times \mathcal{S} \to [0,1]$ is the
        \textbf{transition probability function}, where $P(s' \mid s, a)$ denotes
        the probability of transitioning to state $s'$ after taking action $a$ in
        state $s$;
  \item $R: \mathcal{S} \times \mathcal{A} \to \mathbb{R}$ is the \textbf{reward
        function}, which encodes the agent's objective (e.g., progress toward the
        goal while penalizing collisions and comfort violations);
  \item $\gamma \in [0, 1)$ is the \textbf{discount factor}, which controls the
        relative weight of immediate versus future rewards.
\end{itemize}
The agent's goal is to find an optimal policy $\pi^*: \mathcal{S} \to \mathcal{A}$
(or a distribution over actions in the stochastic case) that maximizes the
expected discounted cumulative reward
$\mathbb{E}\!\left[\sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \gamma^t R(s_t, a_t)\right]$.
The Markov property---that the next state depends only on the current state and
action, not on the full history---is what makes this optimization tractable via
Bellman recursion.

\subsubsection*{Partially Observable Markov Decision Process}

While the MDP formalism is theoretically elegant, it assumes that the agent
has complete access to the world state $s_t$ at each time step.  This assumption
is violated in virtually every realistic autonomous driving scenario.
Sensor range is finite: a vehicle cannot observe occluded regions behind
buildings or other vehicles.  Sensor resolution is limited: LiDAR point clouds
resolve objects only down to a certain angular precision.  And the world state
includes latent variables that are not directly observable---such as the
\emph{intent} of a pedestrian about to step off the curb, or the
\emph{aggressiveness} of an oncoming merging driver.

The \emph{Partially Observable Markov Decision Process} (POMDP) extends the
MDP to handle this observational uncertainty by adding two components:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item $\mathcal{O}$: an \textbf{observation space}, representing what the
        agent actually perceives (e.g., raw LiDAR scans, bounding-box
        detections, or occupancy grids);
  \item $Z: \mathcal{S} \times \mathcal{A} \times \mathcal{O} \to [0,1]$: an
        \textbf{observation function}, where $Z(o \mid s', a)$ is the probability
        of observing $o$ after transitioning to state $s'$ via action $a$.
\end{itemize}
Because the agent cannot directly observe the true state, the principled
solution is to maintain a \textbf{belief state}
$b_t \in \Delta(\mathcal{S})$---a probability distribution over world states
given the history of observations and actions.  The belief is updated at each
step via Bayes' rule:
\[
  b_{t+1}(s') \;\propto\;
  Z(o_{t+1} \mid s', a_t)\!
  \sum_{s \in \mathcal{S}} P(s' \mid s, a_t)\, b_t(s).
\]
Planning in belief space transforms the POMDP into an MDP over the continuous
belief simplex, which is \textbf{PSPACE-hard} in the finite
horizon~\cite{hubmann2018automated}.  In practice, this intractability is
managed through particle-filter approximations of the belief state, and through
architectures that use recurrent networks (LSTMs or GRUs) or Transformer memory
modules as implicit belief representations, replacing the explicit probability
distribution with a learned latent state.

\citeauthor{hubmann2018automated}~\cite{hubmann2018automated} demonstrate a
concrete instantiation of POMDP planning for autonomous driving at unsignalized
intersections, where the hidden state includes the intent of crossing vehicles.
By planning over a discrete approximation of the belief space, their system can
perform evidence-gathering maneuvers---briefly inching forward to elicit
reactions from occluded drivers---that are impossible for a fully observable MDP
planner.  This example illustrates why the POMDP formalism is not merely a
theoretical refinement but a practically essential one for safety-critical
driving decisions.

\subsection{Multi-Agent Extension: Stochastic Games and Dec-POMDP}
\label{sec:multiagent_formalism}

When multiple agents operate simultaneously in a shared environment and each
agent's reward depends on the joint behavior of all agents, the single-agent
POMDP framework becomes insufficient.  Two distinct formalisms are employed
depending on whether agents share objectives (cooperative settings) or have
conflicting incentives (competitive or mixed-motive settings).

\subsubsection*{Stochastic Games and Markov Games}

A \emph{Stochastic Game} (SG), introduced by
\citeauthor{shapley1953stochastic}~\cite{shapley1953stochastic}, is defined by
the tuple
$\langle N, \mathcal{S}, \{\mathcal{A}^i\}_{i=1}^N, P, \{R^i\}_{i=1}^N, \gamma \rangle$,
where $N$ is the number of agents, each with its own action space
$\mathcal{A}^i$ and reward function
$R^i: \mathcal{S} \times \mathcal{A}^1 \times \cdots \times
\mathcal{A}^N \to \mathbb{R}$.
The transition function $P(s' \mid s, a^1, \ldots, a^N)$ depends on the
\emph{joint} action of all agents.  Stochastic games generalize both
single-agent MDPs (when $N=1$) and matrix games (when $|\mathcal{S}|=1$),
and can represent fully competitive (zero-sum), fully cooperative (team-reward),
or mixed-motive multi-agent interactions.

\citeauthor{littman1994markov}~\cite{littman1994markov} introduced the closely
related framework of \emph{Markov Games}, which has become the dominant
formalism in the MARL literature.  Under this framework, the solution concept
is a \emph{Nash equilibrium} policy profile
$(\pi^{1*}, \ldots, \pi^{N*})$ such that no agent can unilaterally improve its
expected return by deviating from its equilibrium policy.  For zero-sum
two-player games, a unique Nash equilibrium in mixed strategies always exists
(guaranteed by minimax theory); for general-sum games with $N > 2$, computing
equilibria is PPAD-hard in the worst case.  In the cooperative case where all
agents share the same reward function ($R^1 = \cdots = R^N = R$), the Markov
Game reduces to a joint policy optimization problem, directly connecting
game-theoretic and MARL formulations.

In the context of autonomous driving, Markov Games capture the strategic
interplay of traffic participants naturally.  A merging scenario, for instance,
can be modeled as a mixed-motive game: the merging vehicle prefers a gap in
traffic, while the through-lane vehicle prefers not to yield---yet both are
penalized for collision.  \citeauthor{sadigh2016planning}~\cite{sadigh2016planning}
exploit this structure to derive trajectories that deliberately influence the
behavior of the human-driven vehicle by communicating intent through motion,
demonstrating how game-theoretic reasoning can make autonomous vehicles more
cooperative and interpretable traffic partners.

\subsubsection*{Decentralized Partially Observable Markov Decision Process}

For cooperative multi-agent settings with partial observability---the dominant
setting in real-world autonomous driving---the most general formalism is the
\emph{Decentralized Partially Observable Markov Decision Process}
(Dec-POMDP)~\cite{bernstein2002complexity}.  A Dec-POMDP augments the Markov
Game with per-agent observation spaces $\{\mathcal{O}^i\}$ and observation
functions $\{Z^i\}$:
\[
  \langle N, \mathcal{S}, \{\mathcal{A}^i\}, P, R, \{\mathcal{O}^i\}, \{Z^i\}, \gamma \rangle.
\]
Crucially, in the fully cooperative Dec-POMDP, agents share a single team
reward $R$, but each agent $i$ observes only
$o^i_t \sim Z^i(\cdot \mid s_t, a_{t-1})$ rather than the full state.
At execution time, each agent must act based solely on its own local
observation history, without access to other agents' observations or actions.

The key theoretical result is that solving a Dec-POMDP optimally is
\textbf{NEXP-hard}~\cite{bernstein2002complexity}---significantly harder than
the PSPACE-hard single-agent POMDP.  The root cause is the combinatorial
explosion in the space of joint histories: with $N$ agents each accumulating
$T$ time steps of observations, the joint observation-action history space
grows as $O\!\left((|\mathcal{O}|\cdot|\mathcal{A}|)^{N \cdot T}\right)$.
In practice, this intractability is addressed through three complementary
strategies:
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{CTDE approximations}: training a centralized critic with
        access to global state while restricting each actor to local
        observations only, as formalized by
        \citeauthor{kraemer2016multi}~\cite{kraemer2016multi} and later
        instantiated in MADDPG~\cite{lowe2017multi} and QMIX~\cite{rashid2018qmix};
  \item \textbf{Communication}: allowing agents to share compact messages,
        effectively reducing the degree of partial observability at the cost
        of communication bandwidth (see Section~\ref{sec:paradigm2});
  \item \textbf{Value-function factorization}: assuming conditional
        independence across subsets of agents to decompose the joint
        Q-function into per-agent utilities
        (VDN~\cite{sunehag2018value}, QMIX~\cite{rashid2018qmix}).
\end{enumerate}

A \textbf{cooperative} fleet of autonomous vehicles---such as platoon control
or cooperative intersection management---is most naturally modeled as a
Dec-POMDP: agents share the objective of maximizing traffic throughput while
minimizing total travel time, but each vehicle can only observe agents within
its sensor range and must act locally at execution time.  A
\textbf{mixed-motive} traffic scenario (e.g., lane merging or yielding at a
roundabout) is better modeled as a Markov Game, where individual reward
functions capture private preferences while a shared collision penalty aligns
agents toward safety.

\subsection{Deep Reinforcement Learning Primer}
\label{sec:drl_primer}

The methods surveyed in later sections build on a small set of core deep
reinforcement learning algorithms.  This subsection provides a self-contained
summary of these building blocks, emphasizing the aspects most relevant to
their multi-agent extensions.

\subsubsection*{Value-Based Methods: DQN and Variants}

\citeauthor{mnih2015human}~\cite{mnih2015human} demonstrated that a
convolutional neural network trained via Q-learning---the \emph{Deep
Q-Network} (DQN)---could achieve superhuman performance on 49 Atari games
from raw pixel input.  DQN stabilizes Q-learning with two key innovations:
an \emph{experience replay buffer} that breaks temporal correlations between
consecutive training samples, and a \emph{target network} that freezes the
bootstrapping parameters for a fixed number of steps, preventing the
divergence caused by moving learning targets.

In the multi-agent context, DQN forms the foundation of value-decomposition
methods.  Both VDN~\cite{sunehag2018value} and QMIX~\cite{rashid2018qmix}
maintain per-agent local $Q$-functions and mix them into a factored joint
Q-value, inheriting the stability properties of experience replay and target
networks while scaling to cooperative teams of agents.

\subsubsection*{Policy Gradient Methods}

While value-based methods work well for discrete action spaces, autonomous
driving typically demands continuous control (e.g., precise steering angle
and throttle values).  Policy gradient methods directly optimize the
parameters $\theta$ of a stochastic policy $\pi_\theta$ by ascending the
gradient of the expected return:
\[
  \nabla_\theta J(\pi_\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\pi_\theta}\!\left[
    \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t) \cdot A(s_t, a_t)\right],
\]
where $A(s_t, a_t) = Q(s_t, a_t) - V(s_t)$ is the advantage function that
centers the gradient signal around a baseline, substantially reducing
variance compared to the raw Monte-Carlo return.

\emph{Asynchronous Advantage Actor-Critic} (A3C)~\cite{mnih2016asynchronous}
parallelizes gradient collection across multiple environment instances, enabling
sample-efficient online updates and decorrelating experience.  \emph{Proximal
Policy Optimization} (PPO)~\cite{schulman2017proximal} further stabilizes
training by clipping the policy update ratio within a trust region:
\[
  \mathcal{L}^{\text{CLIP}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_t\!\left[
    \min\!\left(r_t(\theta)\, A_t,\;
    \mathrm{clip}(r_t(\theta), 1{-}\epsilon, 1{+}\epsilon)\, A_t\right)\right],
\]
where $r_t(\theta) = \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t) / \pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(a_t \mid s_t)$
is the probability ratio and $\epsilon$ is the clip threshold.  PPO's
simplicity, stability, and strong empirical performance have made it the
de facto standard for single-agent continuous-control driving tasks, and it
directly generalizes to the multi-agent setting as MAPPO~\cite{yu2021surprising}.

Off-policy methods offer greater sample efficiency.  \emph{Deep Deterministic
Policy Gradient} (DDPG) extends Q-learning to continuous action spaces by
maintaining a deterministic actor $\mu_\theta(s)$ trained by ascending the
critic gradient $\nabla_\theta Q(s, \mu_\theta(s))$.  \emph{Soft Actor-Critic}
(SAC) adds entropy regularization to the policy objective, encouraging
exploration and preventing premature convergence to deterministic policies---a
property particularly beneficial in sparse-reward driving scenarios where
broad exploration of the state space is critical.

\subsubsection*{Relevance to Multi-Agent Extensions}

Each single-agent algorithm above has a multi-agent analogue:
DQN $\to$ QMIX~\cite{rashid2018qmix} (cooperative value decomposition);
DDPG $\to$ MADDPG~\cite{lowe2017multi} (centralized critic, decentralized
actors); PPO $\to$ MAPPO~\cite{yu2021surprising} (agents with a shared or
centralized critic).  The key challenge in adapting single-agent algorithms
to the multi-agent setting is the \emph{non-stationarity} problem: from
agent $i$'s perspective, the environment's transition distribution
$P(s' \mid s, a^i)$ appears non-stationary because it implicitly depends on
the simultaneously changing policies of all other agents.  This violates the
Markov assumption required for convergence guarantees of standard single-agent
RL algorithms, and motivates the Centralized Training, Decentralized Execution
(CTDE) framework discussed at depth in Section~\ref{sec:paradigm2}.

\subsection{Graph Neural Networks for Relational Reasoning}
\label{sec:gnns}

Traffic scenes are inherently \emph{relational}: the relevance of a
neighboring vehicle to the ego-agent depends on spatial proximity, relative
heading, and the road topology.  Standard convolutional neural networks
process regular grids and lack the inductive biases needed to efficiently
encode these pairwise agent relationships.  \emph{Graph Neural Networks}
(GNNs) provide the appropriate representation: they model a traffic scene as
a directed graph $\mathcal{G} = (\mathcal{V}, \mathcal{E})$ where nodes
$v \in \mathcal{V}$ represent agents (or road elements) and edges
$(u, v) \in \mathcal{E}$ encode spatial or semantic interactions.
Node features are iteratively updated via message passing:
\[
  h_v^{(k+1)} = \mathrm{UPDATE}\!\left(h_v^{(k)},\;
    \mathrm{AGGREGATE}\!\left(\{m_{uv}^{(k)} : (u,v) \in \mathcal{E}\}\right)\right),
\]
where $m_{uv}^{(k)}$ is a message from neighbor $u$ to $v$ at layer $k$.

\emph{Graph Attention Networks} (GAT)~\cite{velickovic2018graph} extend this
framework by computing learned attention coefficients $\alpha_{uv}$ that
weight each neighbor's contribution:
\[
  \alpha_{uv} = \frac{
    \exp\!\left(\mathrm{LeakyReLU}(\mathbf{a}^\top
      [\mathbf{W} h_u \,\|\, \mathbf{W} h_v])\right)
  }{
    \sum_{k \in \mathcal{N}(v)} \exp\!\left(\mathrm{LeakyReLU}(\mathbf{a}^\top
      [\mathbf{W} h_k \,\|\, \mathbf{W} h_v])\right)
  },
\]
where $\mathbf{W}$ is a learnable projection matrix, $\mathbf{a}$ is an
attention vector, and $\|$ denotes vector concatenation.  The attention
mechanism allows the network to focus automatically on the most relevant
neighboring agents---such as the lead vehicle in the ego-lane during a
car-following maneuver---without requiring hand-crafted distance thresholds
or fixed neighborhood sizes.

\citeauthor{jiang2019graph}~\cite{jiang2019graph} apply GNNs directly to
cooperative MARL, constructing a dynamic graph from agent observations and
using graph convolutions to compute communication-like feature aggregations.
Their Graph Convolutional Reinforcement Learning approach demonstrates that
policies learned over a graph adjacency structure are naturally invariant to
permutations of agent indices---a critical inductive bias when the number of
surrounding vehicles changes dynamically across episodes.

In the trajectory prediction domain, \citeauthor{salzmann2020trajectron}
\cite{salzmann2020trajectron} (Trajectron++) and
\citeauthor{liang2020lanegcn}~\cite{liang2020lanegcn} (LaneGCN) demonstrate
that GNNs encoding heterogeneous agent-to-agent and agent-to-map interactions
substantially outperform architectures based on rasterized top-down images,
both in displacement error and in the calibration of predicted probability
distributions.

This subsection provides the representational foundation for the
value-decomposition and communication methods reviewed in
Section~\ref{sec:paradigm2}: QMIX, graph-based MARL, and VectorNet all rely
on variants of graph-structured feature aggregation to encode inter-agent
dependencies compactly.

\subsection{Overview of the Autonomous Driving Software Stack}
\label{sec:ad_stack}

Understanding where learning-based methods intervene in the broader autonomous
driving pipeline is essential for contextualizing the papers reviewed in
subsequent sections.  Classical autonomy systems adopt a \emph{modular
pipeline} architecture consisting of four sequentially composed sub-systems:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, label=\textbf{(\arabic*)}]
  \item \textbf{Perception.}
        Raw sensor data (LiDAR point clouds, camera images, radar returns)
        are processed to detect and classify objects (vehicles, pedestrians,
        cyclists), estimate their 3-D poses, and build a semantic map of the
        scene.  While deep learning has largely solved static-object detection,
        multi-sensor fusion under adverse weather and detection under adversarial
        conditions remain active research areas.

  \item \textbf{Prediction.}
        Given the current perceived state, the prediction module forecasts
        the short-horizon ($\leq$5 s) future trajectories of all road users.
        This is the module most directly connected to the interaction-modeling
        literature surveyed in Section~\ref{sec:paradigm2}: Social
        GAN~\cite{gupta2018social}, Trajectron++~\cite{salzmann2020trajectron},
        VectorNet~\cite{gao2020vectornet}, and TNT~\cite{zhao2020tnt} all
        operate at this layer.

  \item \textbf{Planning.}
        Given predicted futures, the planner selects a reference trajectory
        or sequence of waypoints for the ego-vehicle that optimizes the driving
        objective (efficiency, comfort, safety) while respecting traffic rules
        and predicted agent behaviors.  MARL methods primarily intervene at this
        layer: cooperative policy networks replace or augment the traditional
        motion planner, enabling dynamic negotiation of right-of-way and
        adaptive formation control.

  \item \textbf{Control.}
        A low-level controller (PID, MPC, or neural feedback policy) tracks the
        planned trajectory by issuing throttle, braking, and steering commands
        to the vehicle actuators.
\end{enumerate}

The modular pipeline's principal weakness is the \emph{cascading error}
problem: imperfections at any stage compound as they propagate downstream, and
the planning module receives no gradient signal that would allow it to request
more uncertainty-aware predictions.  Furthermore, the pipeline's clean
separation of concerns breaks down whenever prediction and planning are tightly
coupled, as in the merged prediction-planning paradigm of PiP~\cite{song2020pip}.

\emph{End-to-end} (E2E) learning approaches collapse the entire pipeline into
a single neural network that maps raw observations directly to control
commands~\cite{bojarski2016end,codevilla2018end}.  E2E architectures eliminate
the information bottleneck between modules and allow the system to learn
task-relevant representations directly from driving objectives.  However, E2E
methods trade interpretability for performance and raise significant challenges
for safety certification.  \citeauthor{sallab2017deep}~\cite{sallab2017deep}
propose a recurrent deep RL architecture positioned between these extremes:
it uses learned perceptual features but trains a separate policy module via RL
rather than pure imitation, gaining flexibility without discarding modularity
entirely.

MARL methods can be positioned along this modular-to-end-to-end spectrum.
Value-decomposition approaches such as QMIX~\cite{rashid2018qmix} typically
operate at the planning level, receiving pre-processed observation vectors
from an upstream perception stack.  Communication-based methods like
TarMAC~\cite{das2019tarmac} add a lateral information-sharing channel between
the planning modules of multiple vehicles.  LLM-based cognitive agents
(Section~\ref{sec:paradigm3}) operate at the highest level of abstraction,
producing natural-language driving instructions that are translated into motion
primitives by downstream controllers.

Together, the formal frameworks (MDP/POMDP/SG/Dec-POMDP), algorithmic
building blocks (DQN, PPO, DDPG, actor-critic), representational tools (GNNs),
and architectural context (the AD software stack) established in this section
provide the essential scaffolding for the three-paradigm survey that follows.

\section{Paradigm I: Single-Agent Learning Foundations}
\label{sec:paradigm1}

The first paradigm in the evolution of learning-based autonomous driving
(approximately 2016--2018) established the core machine-learning machinery
that all subsequent multi-agent methods build upon.  During this period,
researchers demonstrated that deep neural networks could learn end-to-end
driving policies directly from raw sensory input, that reinforcement learning
could optimize policies in simulated traffic without hand-engineered reward
functions, and that interaction-aware trajectory prediction models could
forecast the short-horizon futures of neighboring road users.  These
contributions were transformative.  Yet they shared a common, fundamental
blind spot: each method treated the traffic environment as if it contained
only a single learning agent, ignoring the strategic interdependence between
co-evolving policies.  This section surveys the key methods of Paradigm~I,
assessing both their technical contributions and the precise limitations that
motivated the multi-agent methods of Paradigm~II.

% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Imitation Learning and Behavior Cloning}
\label{sec:imitation}

\subsubsection{End-to-End Learning: DAVE-2 and Successors}

The dominant paradigm of the pre-deep-learning era decomposed driving into a
pipeline of hand-engineered modules.  The landmark work of
\citeauthor{bojarski2016end}~\cite{bojarski2016end} (the NVIDIA DAVE-2 system)
challenged this paradigm with a radical simplification: a nine-layer
convolutional neural network trained end-to-end to map raw front-facing camera
images directly to a continuous steering command.  Trained on approximately
72 hours of human driving data, DAVE-2 successfully navigated roads with
explicit lane markings, roads without lane markings, and even parking lots in
experiments, demonstrating that a single feed-forward network could implicitly
learn the full perception-to-control mapping without any intermediate
supervision.

The contribution of DAVE-2 was primarily proof of concept: it established that
deep imitation learning for driving was feasible at all.  Three concrete
technical advantages emerged from the end-to-end formulation.  First, the
network could learn task-relevant features automatically, without the need to
specify which visual cues correspond to lane boundaries or upcoming obstacles.
Second, training was data-efficient in the sense that the same labeled examples
simultaneously improved all components of the pipeline, rather than requiring
separate annotation budgets for perception, prediction, and planning.  Third,
the absence of hand-crafted intermediate representations eliminated the
information bottleneck inherent in multi-stage pipelines.

\citeauthor{codevilla2018end}~\cite{codevilla2018end} extended this paradigm
with \emph{Conditional Imitation Learning} (CIL), addressing the crucial
limitation that a pure regression from image to steering cannot resolve the
ambiguity of navigational goals.  At an intersection, for instance, the optimal
steering command is entirely determined by whether the destination lies ahead,
to the left, or to the right---information absent from any single camera frame.
CIL conditions the policy on a high-level navigational command (``turn left'',
``go straight'', ``turn right'', ``follow road'') provided by a route planner,
using a branched network architecture where each branch is activated only when
the corresponding command is issued.  On the CARLA simulator benchmark, CIL
substantially outperformed unconditional imitation baselines, establishing the
conditional architecture as the standard recipe for end-to-end urban driving.

Despite these advances, end-to-end imitation learning suffers from a
structural weakness known as \emph{covariate shift}~\cite{bansal2018chauffeur}.
A behavior-cloned policy is trained exclusively on states visited by the expert
demonstrator, but at test time the agent's own imperfect actions progressively
deviate from the expert's trajectory, leading the agent into novel states
$s \notin \mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}$ for which the cloned policy has no
reliable prediction.  Small errors compound: a slight drift to the right of the
lane at time $t$ places the agent in an out-of-distribution state at time
$t+1$, and the policy---never trained to recover from such states---may respond
with an erratic command that amplifies the error further.  In the single-agent
setting, this is already a critical failure mode; in multi-agent traffic, where
the state space is enlarged by the behaviors of surrounding vehicles, the
covariate shift problem becomes dramatically more severe.

\subsubsection{Addressing Covariate Shift: DAgger and Data Augmentation}

The theoretical solution to covariate shift is \emph{Dataset Aggregation}
(DAgger), introduced by \citeauthor{ross2011reduction}~\cite{ross2011reduction}.
DAgger is an iterative on-policy imitation learning algorithm: at each round,
the current learned policy $\hat{\pi}_t$ is rolled out in the environment,
collecting a dataset of states $\mathcal{D}_t$ actually visited by the
(imperfect) learner.  Expert labels are then queried for those visited states,
and the policy is retrained on the growing aggregate dataset
$\mathcal{D}_1 \cup \cdots \cup \mathcal{D}_t$.  By ensuring that the
training distribution matches the \emph{learner's} state distribution rather
than the expert's, DAgger provides a no-regret guarantee: the cumulative
cost of the learned policy converges to that of the best policy in the
hypothesis class.  The catch is that DAgger requires an interactive expert
capable of labeling any state the learner might visit---expensive in terms of
human annotation or oracle computation.

\citeauthor{bansal2018chauffeur}~\cite{bansal2018chauffeur} addressed the same
problem at industrial scale in \emph{ChauffeurNet}, trained on over 30 million
labeled driving examples from Waymo's real-world fleet.  Their key finding was
counterintuitive: even at this scale, a policy trained on clean expert
demonstrations failed to generalize to novel test scenarios.  Recovery from
suboptimal states---a near-collision, a vehicle temporarily off the lane
center---could not be learned from demonstrations that never showed such
situations.  The solution was \emph{perturbation-based data augmentation}: the
training data was synthetically corrupted by injecting random perturbations into
the rendered bird's-eye-view inputs, and the expert labels were queried for
these perturbed states.  ChauffeurNet operates on a mid-level representation
(a top-down rendering of the scene including agent boxes, road structure, and
traffic lights) rather than raw pixels, making it possible to generate
perturbed variants analytically.  The resulting policy demonstrated markedly
improved robustness to distributional shift and was shown to transfer to
real-vehicle trials.

A critical limitation of ChauffeurNet and DAgger-style approaches persists,
however: the perturbations are applied in isolation, treating the ego-vehicle
as the only actor whose state can deviate from nominal.  In reality, the
distribution of \emph{other} vehicles' states---the speed of the car ahead
that is braking unexpectedly, the trajectory of a pedestrian who begins to
cross at an atypical moment---is equally unconstrained at test time.  No
synthetic augmentation scheme that treats only the ego-vehicle as a random
variable can capture the full diversity of multi-agent traffic interaction.
The fundamental gap is architectural: behavior cloning models a policy
$\pi(a \mid o)$ that responds to observations of other agents but does not
\emph{reason about} them as co-equal agents pursuing their own objectives.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Synthesis: imitation learning as a converging sequence of
patches.}  The progression from DAVE-2 to CIL to DAgger to ChauffeurNet can
be read as a series of increasingly targeted patches on the same root failure.
DAVE-2 demonstrated end-to-end viability but exposed covariate shift.
DAgger patched covariate shift by matching training and test state distributions,
but required an interactive expert.  ChauffeurNet patched the interactive-expert
requirement with synthetic augmentation, but revealed that the patch is
fundamentally incomplete when other agents are present.  CIL patched goal
ambiguity at intersections.  Crucially, each patch leaves the core single-agent
assumption untouched: the surrounding traffic is always treated as exogenous
context, never as co-learning agents.  This is the precise failure mode that
MARL methods of Paradigm~II address by replacing the single-agent policy
gradient objective with a joint or decomposed multi-agent formulation.

% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Reinforcement Learning for Single-Agent Driving}
\label{sec:rl_single}

Reinforcement learning removes the requirement for expert demonstrations by
optimizing the policy directly against a reward signal.  The agent improves
through trial-and-error interaction with the environment, discovering
behaviors that maximize cumulative reward without supervision from
demonstrations.

\citeauthor{sallab2017deep}~\cite{sallab2017deep} proposed one of the earliest
applications of deep RL to autonomous driving, combining a recurrent
architecture (LSTM-augmented policy network) with an asynchronous actor-critic
training scheme.  The recurrent state allowed the policy to maintain an
implicit belief over the full driving history, partially compensating for the
limited field of view of a single camera.  Their framework was evaluated in a
lane-keeping and lane-change scenario in a simulated highway environment,
demonstrating that policies trained with deep RL could match and exceed the
performance of rule-based baselines without any explicit traffic rules being
programmed.

For continuous-action driving tasks---where the output is a real-valued
steering angle and throttle rather than a discrete maneuver selection---
Proximal Policy Optimization~\cite{schulman2017proximal} emerged as the
dominant training algorithm due to its balance of sample efficiency, stability,
and ease of implementation.  Unlike TRPO, which solves a constrained
optimization at each step, PPO enforces the trust-region constraint
approximately via a clipped surrogate objective, making it computationally
tractable for long-horizon driving rollouts.  PPO-trained policies for
lane-following, intersection navigation, and roundabout negotiation in CARLA
became the standard single-agent baselines against which multi-agent methods
are later evaluated.

\citeauthor{kahn2017uncertainty}~\cite{kahn2017uncertainty} addressed an
orthogonal limitation of standard deep RL for driving: the inability to reason
about uncertainty in the learned value function.  Standard model-free RL
explores aggressively, which is acceptable in video-game environments but
catastrophic in physical or realistically simulated driving.  Their
\emph{PLATO} system maintained an ensemble of neural network dynamics models,
using the disagreement between ensemble members as a proxy for epistemic
uncertainty.  A safety-constrained planner avoided actions that led to
high-uncertainty states, dramatically reducing collision rates during the
exploration phase.  This work anticipated the broader \emph{safe RL}
literature that became crucial once MARL methods were deployed in real traffic.

\citeauthor{chen2019model}~\cite{chen2019model} tackled the structural
complexity of urban driving by decomposing the policy into hierarchical
sub-tasks.  Their model-free hierarchical RL framework trained a high-level
meta-controller to select among driving primitives (``change to left lane'',
``follow current lane'', ``decelerate'') and a set of low-level primitive
controllers to execute each selected behavior.  This temporal abstraction
reduced the effective planning horizon for each sub-policy and allowed
different primitives to be trained in targeted scenarios rather than requiring
all behaviors to emerge from a single flat policy.  The approach achieved
substantially better performance on urban intersection scenarios with
uncontrolled traffic in CARLA compared to flat PPO baselines, at the cost of
requiring manual specification of the primitive taxonomy.

Despite these advances, single-agent RL methods share a critical structural
limitation when deployed in multi-vehicle environments: from any individual
agent's perspective, other vehicles are part of the environment, and if those
vehicles are also learning agents (as in any MARL training run), the transition
function $P(s' \mid s, a)$ experienced by the ego-agent is \emph{non-stationary}.
As the policies of surrounding vehicles update during training, the
distribution of their actions---and hence the joint transition distribution---
shifts continuously.  This violates the stationarity assumption underlying
all convergence guarantees for single-agent RL algorithms.  The empirical
consequence is well-documented: single-agent PPO policies that perform
excellently in scenarios with scripted, rule-based traffic participants
degrade significantly when evaluated against other trained-RL agents, because
the behavioral distribution of those agents was never seen during training.

\subsection{Interaction-Aware Trajectory Prediction (Single-Agent Perspective)}
\label{sec:prediction_single}

In parallel with the policy-learning literature, a distinct research thread
focused on improving the \emph{prediction} of other agents' future trajectories
from the perspective of a single observing ego-vehicle.  Accurate trajectory
prediction is a prerequisite for safe planning: the ego-agent needs short-horizon
forecasts of surrounding agents to evaluate whether a given maneuver (e.g.,
a lane change) will produce a collision.

The seminal work in this direction is Social LSTM by
\citeauthor{alahi2016social}~\cite{alahi2016social}, which modeled pedestrian
trajectory prediction in crowded spaces.  A key contribution was the
\emph{Social Pooling} mechanism: rather than predicting each pedestrian's
trajectory independently, the LSTM hidden states of all pedestrians in a
spatial neighborhood were pooled together into a joint context vector that
informed each individual's prediction.  This enabled the model to capture
interaction-induced behaviors such as collision avoidance and group formation.
In the driving context, the grid-based pooling was adapted to model how
neighboring vehicles' current velocities and headings influence the ego-vehicle's
trajectory distribution.  \citeauthor{deo2018convolutional}~\cite{deo2018convolutional}
refined this approach with \emph{Convolutional Social Pooling}, replacing the
fixed-grid occupancy representation with a learned convolutional aggregation
that better captured spatial structure in highway merging scenarios.

\citeauthor{lee2017desire}~\cite{lee2017desire} (DESIRE) introduced a
more powerful generative modeling framework for trajectory prediction,
combining a conditional variational autoencoder (CVAE) with a recurrent
inverse RL component.  DESIRE generates a diverse set of candidate future
trajectories using the CVAE's latent code and then ranks them with a
learned scoring function that evaluates social acceptability and adherence
to the inferred reward function.  The key advance over Social LSTM was the
\emph{multi-modal} output: rather than predicting a single most-likely
trajectory, DESIRE explicitly samples from a distribution of plausible
futures, which is essential when agents face genuine decision ambiguity
(e.g., whether to merge before or after an approaching vehicle).

\citeauthor{gupta2018social}~\cite{gupta2018social} (Social GAN) pushed the
multi-modal paradigm further by replacing the CVAE with a generative
adversarial network (GAN).  The generator samples diverse trajectory
proposals conditioned on a \emph{Global Pooling} mechanism that aggregates
all agents' encoded histories into a single scene-level feature vector.
A discriminator is trained to distinguish generated trajectories from
ground-truth ones, enforcing social plausibility of the output distribution.
Social GAN was among the first works to demonstrate that diversity of
predicted trajectories could be evaluated quantitatively via metrics such as
the \emph{Minimum over $K$ samples} displacement error (minFDE-$K$), which
evaluates how close the best of $K$ generated trajectories is to the
ground truth.  This metric subsequently became a standard benchmark
across the prediction literature.

A critical limitation shared by Social LSTM, DESIRE, and Social GAN is the
\emph{prediction-planning disconnect}: all three systems predict the
trajectories of surrounding agents assuming the \emph{ego-agent's trajectory
is fixed}.  In reality, the ego-vehicle's own planned action influences
what neighboring agents will do.  A vehicle planning to brake suddenly
elicits very different behaviors from the vehicle behind it than a vehicle
planning to accelerate.  This bidirectional coupling---prediction depends on
planning, planning depends on prediction---cannot be captured by any model
that treats prediction as a feed-forward module upstream of a separate planner.
The merged prediction-planning paradigm (PiP~\cite{song2020pip}) that addresses
this coupling appears only in Paradigm~II, once the representational tools
(VectorNet, graph neural networks) for encoding it compactly became available.

\subsection{Critical Analysis: Limitations Driving Paradigm~II}
\label{sec:paradigm1_limits}

The methods surveyed in this section collectively constitute Paradigm~I:
a set of powerful tools, each optimal within its problem formulation, but
each resting on a single-agent assumption that breaks down systematically
in dense, interactive traffic.  Three concrete failure modes emerged clearly
from the literature and directly motivated the MARL methods of Paradigm~II.

\textbf{(1) Covariate shift in imitation learning.}
Behavior-cloned policies are trained on expert state distributions but
evaluated on their own (drifting) state distributions.  DAgger and
perturbation-based augmentation mitigate but do not eliminate this gap;
crucially, neither addresses the co-evolution of surrounding agents'
states.  The structural fix---learning from a reward signal via RL rather
than pure imitation, or from diverse multi-agent interaction data---is the
entry point for Paradigm~II's policy-gradient and CTDE methods.

\textbf{(2) Non-stationarity in single-agent RL.}
Deploying a single-agent RL policy in a multi-vehicle environment makes
the transition function non-stationary from the ego-agent's perspective.
Convergence guarantees evaporate, and policies trained against scripted
opponents fail to generalize to adaptive opponents.  The CTDE framework
(Section~\ref{sec:paradigm2})---in which a centralized critic conditions
on the joint action of all agents during training while decentralized
actors act locally at execution---resolves non-stationarity at training
time by explicitly acknowledging the multi-agent structure of the value
function.

\textbf{(3) Prediction-planning disconnect.}
Single-agent perspective trajectory predictors model other agents as
external stochastic processes, ignoring the ego-vehicle's reactive
influence on those processes.  This is acceptable for distant agents
or low-traffic-density scenarios, but fails catastrophically in
tight merging and yielding interactions where agents continuously
update their intentions in response to each other.  The bidirectional
prediction-planning methods of Paradigm~II (PiP~\cite{song2020pip},
game-theoretic planners~\cite{sadigh2016planning,fisac2019hierarchical})
address this coupling directly.

Together, these three failure modes define the problem that Paradigm~II
sets out to solve: endowing autonomous vehicles with a genuinely
\emph{multi-agent} view of traffic, in which all participants are treated
as co-equal agents with evolving policies rather than as independently
sampled stochastic obstacles.

\section{Paradigm II: Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning --- Algorithms and Coordination}
\label{sec:paradigm2}

Paradigm~II (approximately 2017--2022) is the technical core of this survey.
It spans the period during which the research community confronted the three
failure modes identified in Paradigm~I---covariate shift, non-stationarity,
and the prediction-planning disconnect---and developed a systematic toolkit
to address them.  The organizing principle is the recognition that traffic is
not a single-agent control problem but a \emph{multi-agent game}, and that
building policies for it requires algorithms that explicitly model the joint
distribution of actions, rewards, and beliefs of all participants.

This section is organized thematically.  Subsection~\ref{sec:marl_alg} surveys
the MARL algorithm families, from independent baselines through centralized
training schemes, role-based and hierarchical methods, to game-theoretic
formulations.  Subsection~\ref{sec:comm} examines communication and coordination
protocols.  Subsection~\ref{sec:representation} covers interaction-aware
representation and prediction-planning integration.
Subsection~\ref{sec:ad_apps} surveys the principal autonomous driving
applications.  Subsection~\ref{sec:sim} reviews the simulation and benchmark
ecosystem.  Subsection~\ref{sec:paradigm2_limits} closes with a critical
analysis of the limitations that motivate Paradigm~III.

\subsection{Multi-Agent RL Algorithms}
\label{sec:marl_alg}

\subsubsection{Independent Learning and Its Limitations}

The simplest approach to multi-agent reinforcement learning applies a
standard single-agent algorithm to each agent independently, treating all
other agents as part of the environment.  \citeauthor{tan1993multi}~%
\cite{tan1993multi} formalized this as \emph{Independent Q-Learning} (IQL),
showing that independent learners can converge in stationary settings and
benefit from parameter sharing when agents are homogeneous.  In the autonomous
driving context, parameter-shared IQL is appealing because the same network
can represent the policies of all vehicles, reducing the parameter count and
implicitly regularizing behavior toward a common driving style.

However, IQL inherits a fundamental theoretical deficiency when agents learn
simultaneously: from each agent's perspective, the environment is
\emph{non-stationary} because the policies of other agents change during
training.  The Markov property is violated---the same state-action pair may
yield different returns at different times---and the convergence guarantees of
Q-learning no longer hold.  \citeauthor{de2020independent}~\cite{de2020independent}
studied Independent PPO (IPPO), a proximal policy optimization variant of the
same paradigm, finding empirically that IPPO is surprisingly competitive with
CTDE methods on cooperative benchmarks despite the theoretical
non-stationarity concern.  This finding is significant: it suggests that
parameter sharing and the on-policy regularization provided by the PPO clip
objective act as de facto stabilizers, even without explicit joint-policy
modeling.  The limitation remains, however, when agents are explicitly
adversarial or in mixed-motive scenarios, where the changing policies of
other agents can be adversarially exploited.

\subsubsection{CTDE: Centralized Training, Decentralized Execution}

The dominant design philosophy for cooperative MARL is \emph{Centralized
Training with Decentralized Execution} (CTDE), articulated formally by
\citeauthor{kraemer2016multi}~\cite{kraemer2016multi}.  The key insight is
that during training in simulation, the joint state---including the private
observations and actions of all agents---is fully available to a centralized
critic or mixing function.  This centralized component can condition on the
true joint action-value function $Q(\mathbf{s}, \mathbf{a})$ or the global
state $s$, eliminating non-stationarity at training time.  At execution, each
decentralized actor uses only its local observation $o^i$, which is the
physically realistic constraint for deployed autonomous vehicles that cannot
share arbitrary information instantaneously.

\paragraph{Value decomposition methods.}
\citeauthor{sunehag2018value}~\cite{sunehag2018value} introduced the
\emph{Value Decomposition Network} (VDN), which decomposes the joint
action-value function as a sum of per-agent utilities:
\[
  Q_{\text{tot}}(\mathbf{s}, \mathbf{a}) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} Q^i(o^i, a^i).
\]
Credit assignment to individual agents is solved implicitly: the gradient of
$Q_{\text{tot}}$ with respect to each agent's parameters flows cleanly through
the additive structure.  The limitation of VDN is its linearity: additive
decomposition cannot represent joint action-value functions where the value of
an agent's action depends on what other agents simultaneously do.

\citeauthor{rashid2018qmix}~\cite{rashid2018qmix} addressed this with
\emph{QMIX}, which replaces the additive mixing with a monotone mixing
network---a hypernetwork conditioned on the global state $s$ that combines
per-agent $Q^i$ values using non-negative weights, enforcing the
\emph{Individual-Global-Max} (IGM) condition:
\[
  \arg\max_{\mathbf{a}} Q_{\text{tot}} = \left(
    \arg\max_{a^1} Q^1, \;\ldots,\; \arg\max_{a^n} Q^n
  \right).
\]
IGM guarantees that decentralized greedy action selection is globally optimal.
QMIX substantially outperforms VDN and IQL on the StarCraft Multi-Agent
Challenge~\cite{samvelyan2019starcraft} and became the de facto standard
cooperative MARL baseline.  Its principal limitation is that the monotonicity
constraint is strictly weaker than full expressiveness: there exist joint
Q-functions that satisfy the IGM condition but cannot be represented by any
monotone mixing network.

\citeauthor{son2019qtran}~\cite{son2019qtran} (QTRAN) relaxed the monotonicity
requirement by instead directly learning the joint $Q_{\text{tot}}$ alongside
the factored utilities, penalizing deviations via auxiliary loss terms.  QTRAN
is theoretically more expressive than QMIX but suffers from optimization
instability in practice: the constraint relating the factored and joint value
estimates must be enforced softly, and the resulting saddle-point optimization
is difficult to tune.

\citeauthor{mahajan2019maven}~\cite{mahajan2019maven} (MAVEN) diagnosed a
different failure mode of QMIX: \emph{monotone mixing discourages exploration}.
Because the mixing network must be non-negative, each agent's contribution to
the joint value is always positive, leading to policies that over-exploit known
high-value behaviors and under-explore.  MAVEN introduces a shared latent
variable drawn from a learned prior, conditioning the per-agent networks and
the mixing weights on this variable.  A mutual information objective encourages
diverse behaviors conditioned on different latent realizations, improving
coverage of the state space during training.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Synthesis: value decomposition as a causal chain.}
The four value decomposition methods form a precise causal chain in which each
work identifies and fixes a specific failure mode of its predecessor.
VDN's \emph{linearity} prevents it from modeling agent synergies, motivating
QMIX's state-conditioned monotone mixing.  QMIX's \emph{monotonicity
constraint} prevents it from representing the full class of factorable joint
Q-functions, motivating QTRAN's unconstrained factorization.  QTRAN's
\emph{optimization instability} (arising from softly enforced constraints)
and QMIX's \emph{exploration collapse} together motivate MAVEN's
commitment-variable approach.  This is not a story of incremental
improvement but of each team correctly diagnosing the precise theoretical
deficiency of the prior work and constructing a targeted remedy.  Crucially,
expressiveness and tractability trade off sharply: VDN is trivially stable
but severely underfits complex coordination tasks, while QTRAN is theoretically
complete but empirically fragile.  QMIX strikes the dominant practical
balance, remaining the most widely adopted CTDE baseline in traffic-related
MARL experiments to date.

\paragraph{Actor-critic CTDE methods.}
\citeauthor{lowe2017multi}~\cite{lowe2017multi} introduced \emph{MADDPG},
adapting the deterministic policy gradient framework to mixed cooperative-competitive
settings.  Each agent $i$ maintains a separate centralized critic
$Q^i(\mathbf{o}, \mathbf{a})$ that conditions on all agents' observations and
actions during training, while the actor $\pi^i(a^i \mid o^i)$ uses only local
information at execution.  MADDPG was the first method to demonstrate stable
learning in particle-world cooperative and competitive tasks with continuous
action spaces.  Its limitation---which it shares with all CTDE methods---is
that the size of the joint input to each critic scales with the number of agents,
making the architecture expensive for large fleets.

\citeauthor{foerster2018counterfactual}~\cite{foerster2018counterfactual}
(COMA) tackled the credit assignment problem in cooperative settings using a
\emph{counterfactual baseline}.  The advantage of agent $i$'s action is
estimated as
\[
  A^i(s, \mathbf{a}) = Q(s, \mathbf{a}) - \sum_{a'^i} \pi^i(a'^i \mid o^i)\, Q(s, (\mathbf{a}^{-i}, a'^i)),
\]
where $\mathbf{a}^{-i}$ denotes the actions of all other agents held fixed.
This counterfactual term removes the contribution of agent $i$'s default
behavior from the advantage estimate, enabling clean per-agent credit
assignment even when rewards are shared globally.  COMA is particularly
relevant to traffic scenarios where collisions produce negative rewards shared
by all involved vehicles: the counterfactual baseline correctly attributes
responsibility to the agent whose deviation from its mean behavior caused the
crash.

\citeauthor{yu2021surprising}~\cite{yu2021surprising} (MAPPO) showed that a
straightforward application of PPO in the MARL setting---using a centralized
value function conditioned on the global state and decentralized actors---
performs surprisingly well across a wide range of cooperative benchmarks,
frequently matching or exceeding more sophisticated algorithms.  The ``surprising''
result challenges the prevailing assumption that the special credit assignment
and value decomposition machinery of dedicated CTDE methods is necessary.
MAPPO's limitation is sample efficiency: it is an on-policy algorithm, requiring
fresh rollouts at each update step, which becomes expensive in high-fidelity
traffic simulators.

\citeauthor{kuba2021hatrpo}~\cite{kuba2021hatrpo} (HATRPO/HAPPO) provided the
theoretical underpinning for trust-region methods in cooperative MARL,
proving that a sequentially updating trust-region scheme---where each agent's
policy is updated holding all others fixed---guarantees monotone improvement of
the joint policy under mild assumptions.  This result extends the theoretical
guarantees of TRPO from single-agent to cooperative multi-agent settings.

\citeauthor{peng2021facmac}~\cite{peng2021facmac} (FACMAC) addressed
continuous action spaces in cooperative MARL via \emph{factored multi-agent
centralized policy gradients}, combining the off-policy advantages of DDPG
with a centralized critic that factorizes over agents, enabling gradient-based
joint policy optimization without the combinatorial explosion of a joint
action space.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Synthesis: actor-critic CTDE methods compared.}
Table~\ref{tab:actorcritic} compares the five principal actor-critic CTDE methods

MADDPG~\cite{lowe2017multi} was the proof of concept for the CTDE actor-critic

principled credit assignment.  COMA~\cite{foerster2018counterfactual} solves

action space, limiting its use for fine-grained continuous steering control.
MAPPO~\cite{yu2021surprising} demonstrated that a centralized value function

necessary algorithmic complexity.  HATRPO~\cite{kuba2021hatrpo} is the only
method with monotone improvement guarantees in the multi-agent case, making it
the theoretically preferred baseline where convergence guarantees matter.
FACMAC~\cite{peng2021facmac} is the natural choice when off-policy sample
efficiency is paramount---the dominant requirement in high-fidelity traffic
simulation where each step is computationally expensive.

\subsubsection{Role-Based and Structured MARL}

A recurring observation in multi-agent systems is that agents naturally
specialize: in traffic, trucks, motorcycles, and passenger cars occupy
distinct ecological niches with different performance envelopes and
behavioral conventions.  Role-based MARL formalizes this intuition by
partitioning the agent population into roles and learning role-conditioned
policies.

\citeauthor{wang2021rode}~\cite{wang2021rode} (RODE) decomposes the action
space by first clustering actions into roles based on their effect on the
environment, then learning a role-assignment policy and separate per-role
action policies.  The role assignment is made at a lower temporal frequency
than the action selection, providing temporal abstraction without requiring
manual role definitions.  \citeauthor{wang2020roma}~\cite{wang2020roma}
(ROMA) takes a generative approach, learning role representations by
maximizing the mutual information between role identity and individual behavior
while minimizing the mutual information between role identity and the behaviors
of other agents, encouraging well-separated, internally coherent roles.

In the autonomous driving context, role-based methods are appealing because
heterogeneous agent types do not need to share the same policy network, and
the role structure can encode prior knowledge about driving conventions (e.g.,
emergency vehicles always have right-of-way).  The limitation is
transferability: roles discovered by optimizing one traffic scenario may not
generalize to qualitatively different scenarios, requiring re-discovery of
roles when the environment structure changes.

\subsubsection{Hierarchical MARL}

Hierarchical reinforcement learning decomposes a long-horizon task into a
manager policy that sets subgoals and worker policies that achieve them.  In
the multi-agent extension, hierarchies can be either \emph{within} each agent
(each vehicle has its own high-level goal selector and low-level controller)
or \emph{across} agents (a coordinating meta-controller issues goals to
individual vehicle controllers).

\citeauthor{vezhnevets2017feudal}~\cite{vezhnevets2017feudal} (FeUdal Networks)
established the modern feudal architecture for single-agent hierarchical RL,
using a manager LSTM that produces goal vectors in a latent space and worker
networks that receive intrinsic rewards for following those goals.
\citeauthor{chu2020multi}~\cite{chu2020multi} extended this hierarchy to
multi-agent traffic signal control, demonstrating that a two-level hierarchy---
where a regional coordinator sets phase-duration goals for individual
intersection agents---substantially outperforms flat MARL by decomposing the
optimization into tractable sub-problems.  The limitation of hierarchical MARL
is that the reward signal must be decomposed consistently across levels; poorly
designed sub-goal rewards lead to the \emph{reward hacking} failure mode where
workers achieve subgoals in ways the manager did not intend.

\subsubsection{Game-Theoretic Approaches}

When agents have mixed motives---simultaneous cooperative and competitive
objectives, as in merging and yielding scenarios---game-theoretic methods
provide a principled framework for characterizing equilibrium behavior.

\citeauthor{hu2003nash}~\cite{hu2003nash} (Nash Q-learning) extended Q-learning
to stochastic games by having each agent compute the Nash equilibrium of the
stage game defined by the current joint Q-estimates, using the Nash equilibrium
value as the bootstrap target.  While theoretically grounded, Nash Q-learning
is computationally intractable when the action space is large, because
computing Nash equilibria is PPAD-hard in general.

\citeauthor{foerster2018lola}~\cite{foerster2018lola} (LOLA) took a different
approach: rather than finding an equilibrium of the current game, LOLA trains
agents to \emph{shape} the learning dynamics of their opponents.  By
differentiating through the gradient update of other agents, the LOLA agent
anticipates how its policy changes will influence opponents' future policies,
and selects updates that steer the joint learning trajectory toward mutually
beneficial outcomes.  LOLA successfully learns cooperative behavior in the
iterated Prisoner's Dilemma, a setting where standard gradient descent converges
to mutual defection.  The practical limitation for autonomous driving is the
requirement to differentiate through opponents' update steps, which requires
white-box access to other agents' parameters---unavailable in realistic
multi-vehicle deployment.

\citeauthor{lanctot2017unified}~\cite{lanctot2017unified} (PSRO / Policy Space
Response Oracle) provides a population-level alternative, iteratively computing
best responses against historical opponent populations and using a meta-game
solver to determine the mixture over historical strategies.  PSRO converges
to approximate Nash equilibria in two-player zero-sum games and generalizes
to multi-player settings.

\subsubsection{Population-Based Training and Self-Play}

Population-based training maintains a population of agent copies at different
stages of training, periodically replacing weaker agents with perturbed copies
of stronger ones.  \citeauthor{vinyals2019alphastar}~\cite{vinyals2019alphastar}
demonstrated the power of this approach in AlphaStar, where a league of agents
playing against each other and historical strategies produced superhuman
StarCraft~II performance.  In the autonomous driving context, population-based
training generates a diverse pool of driving behaviors against which the ego
policy must be robust, serving as a principled alternative to hand-scripted
opponent models.  The limitation is computational cost: maintaining a large
policy population and continuously evaluating cross-play performance requires
orders of magnitude more compute than training a single policy.

\subsection{Communication and Coordination Protocols}
\label{sec:comm}

In settings where vehicles can exchange information---via V2V or V2X wireless
links---learning what to communicate, when, and to whom becomes a design
problem of its own.

\subsubsection{Learned Communication: Early Approaches}

\citeauthor{foerster2016learning}~\cite{foerster2016learning} introduced two
foundational learned communication protocols: RIAL (Reinforced Inter-Agent
Learning), where discrete messages are treated as actions optimized by
reinforcement learning, and DIAL (Differentiable Inter-Agent Learning), where
a continuous relaxation allows gradients to flow through the communication
channel during training.  DIAL demonstrated that jointly training
communication and action policies produces qualitatively richer message
conventions than optimizing communication separately.

\citeauthor{sukhbaatar2016learning}~\cite{sukhbaatar2016learning} (CommNet)
simplified the communication architecture by broadcasting each agent's hidden
state to all others and taking the mean as the communication signal.  The mean
pooling is permutation-invariant and handles variable agent counts gracefully.
CommNet showed strong performance on cooperative tasks but suffers from two
limitations in the V2X context: the broadcast model does not respect
communication range limits, and the mean pooling discards information about
\emph{which} agent sent which message, making it impossible to condition
behavior on the identity of communicating neighbors.

\subsubsection{Targeted and Selective Communication}

\citeauthor{das2019tarmac}~\cite{das2019tarmac} (TarMAC) replaced the broadcast
model with \emph{attention-based targeted communication}: each sender produces
a query and a message; each receiver attends over all senders using a
compatibility function between its own key and each sender's query.  This
soft attention mechanism allows agents to selectively ignore messages from
irrelevant neighbors, reducing the effective communication bandwidth while
preserving information from the most relevant sources.

\citeauthor{jiang2018learning}~\cite{jiang2018learning} (ATOC) introduced
explicit \emph{communication gating}: an auxiliary binary classifier decides
whether each agent should initiate communication in the current timestep.
By learning to communicate only when the information gain exceeds a threshold,
ATOC substantially reduces the number of messages exchanged while maintaining
task performance.

\citeauthor{wang2021vbc}~\cite{wang2021vbc} (VBC) extended gated communication
to value-based criteria: an agent initiates a message only when the value
improvement from sharing its hidden state exceeds a learned threshold.  This
approach ties the communication decision directly to the downstream effect on
the Q-function, producing communication policies that are interpretable in
terms of their decision-theoretic rationale.

\citeauthor{singh2019learning}~\cite{singh2019learning} (IC3Net) coupled the
communication gate to the agent's own confidence in its action: when the entropy
of the action distribution is low (the agent is confident), it broadcasts its
hidden state; when entropy is high (the agent is uncertain), it listens.  This
produces emergent coordination where uncertain agents defer to confident ones.

\subsubsection{Graph-Based Communication}

Graph-based communication explicitly models the interaction topology between
agents, restricting message passing to pairs within a given communication
range or connected by a learned link prediction network.

\citeauthor{jiang2019graph}~\cite{jiang2019graph} (Graph Convolutional
Reinforcement Learning) constructs a dynamic agent interaction graph from
spatial proximity, applies graph convolutional message passing at each
timestep, and integrates the resulting agent embeddings into a cooperative
policy.  The topology-aware aggregation allows agents to distinguish signals
from near-range and far-range neighbors, unlike broadcast methods that treat
all agents equally.

\citeauthor{kim2019learning}~\cite{kim2019learning} learns the communication
graph structure itself as a discrete latent variable, using gradient estimators
to backpropagate through the discrete graph-selection step.  The learned graphs
exhibit sparse, structured connectivity---consistent with the intuition that
vehicles most relevant to driving decisions are nearby vehicles in the same lane
or at the upcoming intersection, not arbitrary distant agents.

\subsubsection{Bandwidth-Constrained Communication}

Real V2X channels have finite capacity and are subject to packet loss, latency,
and interference.  \citeauthor{kim2019message}~\cite{kim2019message} studied
the effect of random message dropout, simulating packet loss rates up to 50\%,
and found that cooperative MARL policies trained without dropout degrade sharply
under even moderate loss.  Training with dropout injection produces more robust
policies that effectively hedge against communication failure by learning actions
that are safe under the worst-case assumption of message non-arrival.

\citeauthor{wang2020bandwidth}~\cite{wang2020bandwidth} formulated
bandwidth-constrained communication as an explicit constraint in the policy
optimization, penalizing the total number of bits transmitted per timestep.
By jointly optimizing task reward and bandwidth consumption, their method
produces policies that achieve a Pareto-optimal tradeoff between coordination
performance and communication overhead.

\subsubsection{V2X Cooperative Perception}

The most direct application of multi-agent communication to autonomous driving
is \emph{cooperative perception}: vehicles share sensor data to extend each
other's effective sensing range, improving detection of occluded objects and
increasing the horizon for collision avoidance.

\citeauthor{wang2020v2vnet}~\cite{wang2020v2vnet} (V2VNet) demonstrated that
intermediate feature sharing---each vehicle transmits a compressed spatial
feature map of its local LiDAR scan---substantially improves 3D object detection
compared to ego-only perception, with the greatest gains in occluded regions
where a single sensor cannot see.  Compared to raw-sensor sharing (early fusion),
intermediate fusion is far more bandwidth-efficient because the feature maps
are compact; compared to prediction sharing (late fusion), it retains richer
spatial information.  The practical limitation is that feature representations
must be calibrated to a common reference frame, requiring centimeter-level
localization accuracy and time-synchronized clocks across the communicating
fleet.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Synthesis: communication protocol evolution.}
The six subfields surveyed above form a clear ladder of increasing realism,
each rung motivated by the assumptions that broke the prior rung.
\emph{DIAL/RIAL}~\cite{foerster2016learning} established that communication
is \emph{learnable}, but treats the channel as noise-free and unlimited.
\emph{CommNet}~\cite{sukhbaatar2016learning} demonstrated that
permutation-invariant broadcast can coordinate large teams efficiently,
but its indiscriminate broadcast wastes bandwidth and erases sender identity.
\emph{TarMAC and ATOC}~\cite{das2019tarmac,jiang2018learning} restored
message selectivity via attention gating, reducing transmitted bits while
preserving decision-relevant information.
\emph{Graph-based methods}~\cite{jiang2019graph,kim2019learning} added
spatial topology awareness, restricting messaging to physically plausible
neighbors.
\emph{Bandwidth-constrained methods}~\cite{kim2019message,wang2020bandwidth}
introduced explicit packet-loss robustness and per-bit cost penalties,
bridging the gap between simulation and real V2X deployment.
\emph{V2VNet}~\cite{wang2020v2vnet} moved from policy-level communication
to \emph{perceptual feature sharing}, the most bandwidth-efficient modality
for cooperative detection.
A critical unresolved gap is that all six stages assume
\emph{idealized} communication (bounded latency, known topology, no
authentication overhead), while real V2X protocols (C-V2X, DSRC) introduce
variable latency of 5--200\,ms, intermittent connectivity, and security
overhead that none of these methods are trained to handle.

\subsection{Interaction-Aware Representation and Prediction-Planning Integration}
\label{sec:representation}

The prediction-planning disconnect identified in Paradigm~I is addressed in
Paradigm~II through representational and architectural innovations that
couple the prediction of other agents' futures with the planning of the
ego-vehicle's trajectory.

\subsubsection{GNN-Based Trajectory Prediction}

The development of graph neural networks (Section~\ref{sec:background})
enabled trajectory prediction models that represent the interaction structure
between agents explicitly rather than implicitly through pooling operations.

\citeauthor{salzmann2020trajectron}~\cite{salzmann2020trajectron}
(Trajectron++) models each scene as a heterogeneous directed graph, with nodes
representing agents of different semantic types (pedestrian, bicycle, car,
truck) and edges encoding spatial proximity.  A temporal GNN encodes the
evolving interaction structure, and a CVAE decoder generates multi-modal
trajectory distributions conditioned on the graph embedding.  The heterogeneous
graph structure allows the model to learn different interaction dynamics for
different agent-type pairs (e.g., pedestrian--vehicle interactions differ
qualitatively from vehicle--vehicle interactions), improving prediction accuracy
in mixed-traffic scenarios.

\citeauthor{gao2020vectornet}~\cite{gao2020vectornet} (VectorNet) introduced
a unified vectorized representation for all scene elements---agent trajectories,
lane centerlines, stop lines, crosswalks---encoding each polyline as a set of
directed vectors.  A hierarchical GNN first encodes each polyline locally, then
reasons over polyline interactions globally via a fully-connected attention graph.
VectorNet achieves strong trajectory prediction performance while consuming a
compact, structured scene encoding that scales naturally to scenes with many
lane segments and agents.

\citeauthor{liang2020lanegcn}~\cite{liang2020lanegcn} (LaneGCN) focused
specifically on the road graph structure, applying graph convolutions over the
lane topology graph (nodes are lane segments; edges encode successor,
predecessor, left-neighbor, and right-neighbor relationships).  The
lane-topology-aware encoding captures the ``channel'' structure of road networks
that constrains vehicle motion, substantially improving on unstructured
occupancy-grid baselines.

\citeauthor{zhao2020tnt}~\cite{zhao2020tnt} (TNT) decomposed trajectory
prediction into a two-stage process: first sample a set of target endpoints
conditioned on the map and agent context, then regress a full trajectory to
each sampled target.  The target-conditioned formulation makes the multi-modal
structure explicit---different modes correspond to different intended
destinations---and enables direct supervision of the endpoint distribution.

\citeauthor{chai2019multipath}~\cite{chai2019multipath} (MultiPath) used a
fixed set of $K$ anchors (trajectory prototypes extracted from the training
data via $k$-means) and trained a network to assign a probability mass and a
residual offset to each anchor.  The anchor-based formulation is fast and
deterministic, making it suitable for deployment on embedded hardware.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Synthesis: GNN trajectory prediction as progressive representation refinement.}
The five GNN prediction methods refine scene representation in two orthogonal directions.
Along the \emph{multi-modality} axis: Social LSTM produces a single mean trajectory;
DESIRE adds diversity via CVAE sampling; Social GAN enforces distributional plausibility
via adversarial training; TNT makes multi-modality explicit by conditioning on target endpoints.
Along the \emph{structure} axis: Social LSTM and Social GAN use flat pooling;
Trajectron++ uses heterogeneous agent-type graphs; VectorNet uses a polyline-level
hierarchy that encodes both agent histories and map topology; LaneGCN uses
road-graph topology directly.  The key insight is that these two axes are
largely orthogonal: any multi-modal decoder can in principle be combined with
any structured encoder.  Modern industrial predictors (e.g., Wayformer, MTR)
do exactly this, pairing a VectorNet-style encoder with TNT-style multi-modal
output heads.

A limitation shared by all five methods is that they predict \emph{all agents
independently}, conditioned on each other's \emph{past} motions.  Future
decisions are not modeled as jointly rational: the model for agent $i$ does
not account for the fact that agent $j$'s future trajectory depends on
what agent $i$ decides to do, and vice versa.

\subsubsection{Prediction-Planning Bidirectional Coupling}

\citeauthor{song2020pip}~\cite{song2020pip} (PiP) directly addressed the
one-directional prediction$\rightarrow$planning pipeline by injecting candidate
ego-vehicle plans into the prediction network.  For each hypothetical ego
trajectory $\tau^{\text{ego}}$, the prediction module conditions its output
distribution $P(\tau^j \mid o, \tau^{\text{ego}})$ on the proposed ego plan.
The planning module then selects the ego trajectory that minimizes a composite
cost under the corresponding predicted responses.  This closed loop---planning
informs prediction; prediction informs planning---captures the interactive
structure of merging and yielding without requiring a full game-theoretic
solution.

The limitation of PiP is that the bidirectional coupling is approximated: the
loop is iterated for a fixed number of rounds rather than solved to convergence,
and the prediction model must be retrained for each ego trajectory discretization.
Nevertheless, PiP demonstrated statistically significant improvements over
decoupled prediction-planning on real-world highway merging datasets, validating
the architectural hypothesis.

\subsubsection{Game-Theoretic Motion Planning}

Formal game-theoretic planning frames the motion planning problem as finding
a Nash equilibrium of the joint trajectory game between all road users.

\citeauthor{sadigh2016planning}~\cite{sadigh2016planning} modeled human drivers
as reward-maximizing agents whose reward function is inferred from demonstrations,
enabling an AV planner to predict how human drivers will respond to different
AV trajectory proposals.  The AV selects the trajectory that maximizes its own
reward while accounting for the human's best response.

\citeauthor{fisac2019hierarchical}~\cite{fisac2019hierarchical} coupled
game-theoretic reasoning with formal safety guarantees, using Hamilton--Jacobi
reachability to compute the safe operating envelope and a level-$k$ game model
to anticipate human responses within that envelope.  This hierarchical
safety-aware planning framework was demonstrated in real-vehicle highway
experiments.

\citeauthor{fridovich2020efficient}~\cite{fridovich2020efficient} developed an
efficient iterative linear-quadratic game solver for multi-player continuous
dynamic games, enabling real-time Nash equilibrium computation at the timescales
required for autonomous driving.  Their approach avoids the exponential blowup
of exact Nash computation by alternating between individual best-response
updates linearized around the current joint trajectory, converging to local Nash
equilibria in milliseconds for moderate fleet sizes.

\citeauthor{schwarting2021deep}~\cite{schwarting2021deep} combined social value
orientation (SVO)---a concept from behavioral economics measuring the degree
to which an agent values others' outcomes in addition to its own---with deep
RL, inferring SVO from behavior and conditioning planning on the inferred
social preferences.  This provides a continuous spectrum between fully
cooperative and fully competitive agents, enabling fine-grained modeling of
the social heterogeneity observed in real traffic.

The shared limitation of game-theoretic planners is the assumption of agent
rationality: all models assume that other drivers maximize a well-defined utility
function.  Empirical human driving exhibits substantial irrationality,
inattention, and stylistic diversity that these models cannot capture without
carefully calibrated utility functions.  Furthermore, computing exact Nash
equilibria is intractable for more than a handful of agents, requiring
approximations that may not preserve equilibrium semantics.

% ---- 4.D ----
\subsection{Autonomous Driving Applications of MARL}
\label{sec:ad_apps}

\subsubsection{Traffic Signal Control}

Traffic signal control is the most mature application of MARL to urban mobility.
The state space is well-defined (queue lengths, vehicle counts, phase timers),
the action space is discrete and bounded (phase selection), and the reward
signal is intuitive (minimize total delay).  The multi-agent structure arises
because signals at neighboring intersections interact: greening one phase
releases a platoon that will arrive at the downstream intersection in a
predictable window, and optimal coordination requires anticipating this
coupling.

\citeauthor{wei2019colight}~\cite{wei2019colight} (CoLight) modeled the signal
network as a graph with intersections as nodes and road segments as edges,
applying graph attention convolutions to share state information between
neighboring intersections.  By weighting the influence of each neighbor
according to learned attention coefficients, CoLight adapts the coordination
structure to the local traffic pattern rather than treating all neighbors
equally.  Tested on real-world traffic data from Jinan and Hangzhou city
networks, CoLight reduced average travel time by 9--15\% compared to
the previous state-of-the-art.

\citeauthor{wei2019presslight}~\cite{wei2019presslight} (PressLight) grounded
the reward signal in \emph{max-pressure control theory}, a mathematically
principled queue-balancing heuristic.  By using pressure---defined as the
difference between upstream queue density and downstream capacity---as the
reward signal, PressLight inherits a theoretical throughput guarantee from the
max-pressure literature while allowing RL to optimize for delay and travel
time within that framework.

\citeauthor{zheng2019learning}~\cite{zheng2019learning} (FRAP) introduced a
\emph{factorized phase representation} that explicitly models phase competition:
at each intersection, only phases with non-conflicting vehicle movements can
be active simultaneously.  FRAP encodes this conflict structure into the network
architecture, ensuring that the policy never selects conflicting phases and
simplifying the credit assignment problem by separating independent phase
decisions.

Despite these advances, traffic signal MARL methods share two persistent
limitations.  First, they assume homogeneous vehicle fleets, ignoring priority
vehicles (emergency services, public transit) that violate the standard
queue-delay reward formulation.  Second, the reward function captures
intersection-level delay but does not model network-level externalities such
as the redirection of traffic from optimized corridors to unmanaged side streets.

\subsubsection{Multi-Vehicle Cooperative Driving}

\citeauthor{wang2018urban}~\cite{wang2018urban} applied MARL to cooperative
lane change on multi-lane highways, demonstrating that vehicles trained with
a shared reward that penalizes global travel time learn to create gaps for
merging vehicles rather than closing them selfishly.  The coordination
behavior emerges from the reward structure without any explicit communication;
the key design choice is the shared reward that aligns individual and collective
incentives.

\citeauthor{shalev2016safe}~\cite{shalev2016safe} introduced a
safety-first framework for multi-agent driving, decoupling the safety guarantee
from the performance optimization by constructing a formal model of
``safe distance'' that no trained policy can violate.  The safety filter
acts as a hard constraint on the RL policy's action, correcting any action that
would lead to a foreseeable collision.  This separation of safety and performance
is critical for regulatory acceptance and has influenced subsequent safe MARL
work across the field.

\citeauthor{schwarting2019social}~\cite{schwarting2019social} studied how
autonomous vehicles should behave in the presence of human drivers modeled
with social value orientation, showing that AVs that treat human
drivers as pure game-theoretic opponents can inadvertently \emph{induce}
aggressive behavior through their own assertive actions, while AVs that
account for human social preferences converge to smoother, safer interactions.

\subsubsection{Mixed-Autonomy Traffic}

Mixed-autonomy traffic---scenarios where a minority of AVs share the road
with a majority of human-driven vehicles---poses a unique challenge: the AVs
cannot assume that surrounding vehicles are also optimizing for a common
objective, yet they can exploit the influence AVs exert on human following
behavior to improve global traffic outcomes.

\citeauthor{wu2017flow}~\cite{wu2017flow} developed Flow, an open-source
framework for training RL-based mixed-autonomy traffic controllers in the SUMO
simulator.  Using Flow, the authors demonstrated that a single AV in a ring
road---comprising mostly human-driven vehicles following the IDM car-following
model---can eliminate stop-and-go waves by moderating its own speed, acting
as a mobility regulator for the surrounding traffic.

\citeauthor{stern2018dissipation}~\cite{stern2018dissipation} validated this
finding in a real-world experiment: a single AV inserted into a ring road of
21 human-driven cars reduced total fuel consumption by 40\% and entirely
suppressed the stop-and-go instability observed in the all-human baseline.
This experiment provides one of the most striking empirical demonstrations of
the cooperative leverage that a small number of AVs can exert on aggregate
traffic.

\citeauthor{talebpour2016influence}~\cite{talebpour2016influence} analyzed the
influence of connected AVs on traffic flow stability via an analytical
string-stability argument, showing that even partial penetration rates (as low
as 5\%) are sufficient to dampen disturbances under cooperative adaptive
cruise control, provided that the AV controllers use a sufficiently long
look-ahead horizon.

\subsubsection{Highway and Urban Driving with MARL}

\citeauthor{isele2018navigating}~\cite{isele2018navigating} trained a
MARL policy for navigating complex intersections with occluded traffic,
combining a POMDP-based belief estimator for hidden vehicle states with an
RL policy that conditions on the belief.  The POMDP component provided a
principled treatment of uncertainty about the presence and speed of vehicles
at occluded approaches, while the RL component learned to exploit the
information revealed by cautious creeping maneuvers.

\citeauthor{hubmann2018automated}~\cite{hubmann2018automated} formulated
urban intersection crossing as a Dec-POMDP, using online planning with a
particle filter belief state to handle partial observability.  Their planner
was demonstrated in a physical vehicle experiment, one of the few Paradigm~II
methods evaluated outside of simulation.

\citeauthor{palanisamy2020multi}~\cite{palanisamy2020multi} presented
a comprehensive MARL framework for connected AV fleets trained in CARLA,
comparing MADDPG, MAPPO, and independent baselines across lane change,
intersection, and merging scenarios.  The study confirmed that CTDE methods
consistently outperform independent baselines in dense traffic but require
substantially more training time, reinforcing the efficiency-quality tradeoff
that motivates Paradigm~III's architectural innovations.

\subsection{Simulation Platforms and Benchmarks}
\label{sec:sim}

The empirical progress of Paradigm~II was enabled by a flourishing ecosystem
of open-source simulators and real-world datasets.

\citeauthor{dosovitskiy2017carlaopenurbandriving}~\cite{dosovitskiy2017carlaopenurbandriving}
(CARLA) provides a photorealistic urban driving simulator built on Unreal
Engine, supporting configurable sensor suites (cameras, LiDAR, radar, GPS),
pedestrian and vehicle traffic, weather variation, and a Python API for
multi-agent episode control.  CARLA became the standard training and evaluation
platform for perception-to-control driving policies and MARL experiments
involving heterogeneous sensor-equipped vehicles.

\citeauthor{krajzewicz2012recent}~\cite{krajzewicz2012recent} (SUMO) offers a
fast, open-source, mesoscopic traffic simulator with an extensive network of
real-world road maps importable via OpenStreetMap.  SUMO's speed (capable of
simulating millions of vehicle steps per second) makes it the preferred
platform for large-scale traffic signal MARL and mixed-autonomy experiments
where high-fidelity rendering is less important than simulation throughput.

\citeauthor{zhou2020smarts}~\cite{zhou2020smarts} (SMARTS) was designed
explicitly for multi-agent autonomous driving research, providing a standard
interface for concurrent multi-agent training, diverse scenario composition, and
ego-agent interaction with social vehicles driven by rule-based or other learned
policies.  SMARTS introduced the now-standard multi-agent driving benchmark
suite with canonical evaluation scenarios (cut-in, merge, intersection crossing)
that enable controlled comparison across methods.

\citeauthor{ettinger2021large}~\cite{ettinger2021large} (Waymo Open Motion
Dataset, WOMD) provides 570 hours of real-world driving data from Waymo's
sensor fleet, annotated with 3D object tracks for vehicles, pedestrians, and
cyclists.  The associated motion prediction challenge benchmarks multi-agent
trajectory prediction at the 8-second horizon, becoming the standard competition
for assessing prediction models.

\citeauthor{caesar2020nuscenes}~\cite{caesar2020nuscenes} (nuScenes) and its
closed-loop planning extension \citeauthor{caesar2021nuplan}~\cite{caesar2021nuplan}
(nuPlan) provide 360-degree multi-sensor data from urban driving in Boston and
Singapore.  nuPlan is notable for supporting reactive agent simulation: the
ego vehicle is re-inserted into real logged scenarios and must plan in response
to real traffic participants, with other agents' future trajectories interpolated
from the ground-truth logs.

\citeauthor{chang2019argoverse}~\cite{chang2019argoverse} (Argoverse) introduced
the task of motion forecasting for the autonomous driving community, providing
annotated vehicle and pedestrian tracks alongside high-definition map data.
The Argoverse motion forecasting leaderboard drove substantial progress in
GNN-based prediction during the 2019--2022 period.

\citeauthor{samvelyan2019starcraft}~\cite{samvelyan2019starcraft} (SMAC,
StarCraft Multi-Agent Challenge) is not an autonomous driving platform but
has served as the standard cooperative MARL benchmark on which virtually all
Paradigm~II algorithms (QMIX, QTRAN, MAVEN, COMA, MAPPO) were first evaluated.
The unit micromanagement tasks in StarCraft~II test coordination, credit
assignment, and decentralized execution in a demanding and well-understood
environment.  Reported SMAC results are routinely included alongside driving
results to contextualize algorithm performance in the broader MARL literature.

\subsection{Critical Analysis: Limitations Driving Paradigm~III}
\label{sec:paradigm2_limits}

The Paradigm~II methods surveyed in this section represent a substantial advance
over Paradigm~I.  CTDE algorithms resolve non-stationarity at training time;
communication protocols extend the effective sensing horizon beyond physical
sensors; game-theoretic planners couple prediction and planning;
MARL-trained controllers demonstrate real benefits in traffic signal optimization
and mixed-autonomy regulation.  Yet three systematic limitations prevent
Paradigm~II methods from providing the safety, generalization, and
interpretability required for large-scale autonomous driving deployment.

\textbf{(1) Sample inefficiency and training instability at scale.}
Even with CTDE, training MARL policies in high-fidelity simulation remains
extremely expensive.  Achieving convergence in CARLA scenarios with five or
more interacting learning agents requires tens of millions of environment steps,
implying days of wall-clock training on multi-GPU clusters.  Non-stationarity
is reduced but not eliminated under CTDE when the number of agents is large:
the joint observation space grows combinatorially, and the centralized critic
must generalize over an exponentially larger joint state-action space.
On-policy CTDE methods (COMA, MAPPO) are particularly sample-hungry; off-policy
methods (QMIX, MADDPG, FACMAC) are more efficient but introduce distributional
shift between the replay buffer and the current joint policy.

\textbf{(2) Long-tail generalization failure.}
MARL agents trained on a fixed set of scenarios---even a large and carefully
curated set---generalize poorly to the rare but safety-critical scenarios
that constitute the ``long tail'' of real-world traffic.  An agent trained on
simulated highway merging may perform excellently on standard ramp geometries
but fail on unusual geometry or in the presence of large trucks with atypical
following distances.  The long-tail problem is fundamentally an out-of-distribution
generalization challenge: the joint behavior distribution of all vehicles in
long-tail scenarios was never encountered during training, and the RL policy
has no mechanism to detect that it is operating outside its training distribution.

\textbf{(3) Interpretability deficit.}
Complex MARL policies---particularly those involving attention-based communication
and GNN-based value functions---are opaque black boxes.  Regulatory frameworks
for autonomous vehicles increasingly require explanations of system behavior:
why did the AV brake?  Which sensor data triggered the lane change?  Which
other agent's predicted trajectory influenced the decision?  Paradigm~II
methods provide no principled mechanism for generating such explanations, and
post-hoc attribution techniques developed for supervised learning (SHAP,
integrated gradients) transfer poorly to the non-i.i.d., sequential
decision-making setting.

These three failure modes motivate Paradigm~III (Section~\ref{sec:paradigm3}):
Transformer architectures that improve sample efficiency and global dependency
modeling, and large language models that supply commonsense reasoning for
long-tail scenarios while generating natural-language explanations of agent
decisions.

% ==========================================================
\section{Paradigm III: Transformer-Based and LLM-Augmented MARL}
\label{sec:paradigm3}

Paradigm~III (approximately 2021--present) addresses the two most persistent
failure modes of Paradigm~II---interpretability deficit and long-tail
generalization failure---by importing two complementary architectural
innovations from natural language processing.  Transformer models provide a
principled mechanism for capturing global inter-agent dependencies via
scaled dot-product attention, eliminating the need for hand-crafted
communication graphs and delivering state-of-the-art performance on cooperative
benchmarks.  Large language models (LLMs) encode commonsense knowledge about
traffic conventions, traffic law, and human behavior learned from vast textual
corpora, enabling zero-shot handling of novel scenarios and producing
natural-language reasoning traces that serve as built-in explanations.

\subsection{Transformer Architectures for MARL}

\subsubsection{Sequential Decision Making as Sequence Modeling}

A foundational conceptual shift occurred when
\citeauthor{vaswani2017attention}~\cite{vaswani2017attention} demonstrated
that the Transformer architecture---purely attention-based, without recurrence
or convolution---achieves superior performance on sequence modeling tasks.
The RL community subsequently recognized that trajectories (state, action,
reward triplets over time) are sequences, and that a sufficiently expressive
sequence model conditioned on return-to-go can imitate offline RL without
explicit value function learning.

\citeauthor{chen2021decision}~\cite{chen2021decision} (Decision Transformer)
formalized this idea: the model autoregressively predicts the action at time
$t$ given the sequence
$(\hat{R}_1, s_1, a_1, \hat{R}_2, s_2, a_2, \ldots, \hat{R}_t, s_t)$,
where $\hat{R}_t$ is the desired return from timestep $t$ forward.  By
conditioning on high desired returns at inference time, the model imitates
expert behavior without Bellman backups, eliminating the instability of
off-policy TD learning.  In the autonomous driving context, this is
particularly attractive: large-scale real-world driving logs (Waymo, nuScenes,
Argoverse) can be used directly as offline training data, bypassing the need
for reward annotation beyond basic safety and comfort metrics.

\citeauthor{janner2021offline}~\cite{janner2021offline} (Trajectory Transformer)
extended sequence modeling to the full trajectory distribution, predicting
states and rewards alongside actions and using beam search over the joint
state-action-reward sequence as an implicit planning algorithm.  By treating
planning as inference in a learned trajectory model, the Trajectory Transformer
avoids the compounding errors of model-based rollouts that plague standard
world-model approaches, at the cost of beam search's computational overhead.

\citeauthor{parisotto2020stabilizing}~\cite{parisotto2020stabilizing}
(Gated Transformer-XL, GTrXL) identified a fundamental training instability
when applying standard Transformers to online RL: deep Transformer architectures
are sensitive to the ordering of layer normalization and to the loss of temporal
context across episodes.  GTrXL stabilizes training by combining gating
mechanisms inspired by LSTM with Transformer-XL's segment-level recurrence,
enabling stable credit assignment over long temporal horizons---a key
requirement for urban driving episodes that span dozens of seconds.

The shared limitation of these three methods is that they are fundamentally
\emph{offline}: they require pre-collected, return-labeled datasets and do
not support online refinement through environment interaction without
significant architectural modification.  In the multi-agent setting,
return-labeled offline datasets must include the joint trajectories of all
agents to capture interaction effects, substantially raising the data
collection burden.

\subsubsection{Multi-Agent Transformer}

\citeauthor{wen2022multi}~\cite{wen2022multi} (Multi-Agent Transformer, MAT)
proposed the first Transformer architecture designed specifically for the
cooperative MARL setting.  The key insight is to model the joint action
generation as an \emph{autoregressive sequence} over agents: an encoder
processes all agents' observations jointly via self-attention, producing
contextualized embeddings that capture global dependencies; a decoder then
generates each agent's action conditioned on the joint observations and
the actions of agents processed so far in the sequential ordering.

This encoder-decoder design has two important properties.  First, the encoder
attention computes pairwise agent-to-agent relevance scores, providing an
interpretable measure of which agents are most influential for each agent's
decision---a form of implicit communication without a hand-designed protocol.
Second, the autoregressive decoder makes the conditional action
generation explicit, allowing the model to express complex joint action
distributions (including mode-switching behavior where the optimal action for
agent $i$ changes discontinuously depending on what agent $j$ has chosen)
that factored policies cannot represent.  MAT achieved state-of-the-art
performance on SMAC~\cite{samvelyan2019starcraft} and Google Research Football
benchmarks, outperforming MAPPO and QMIX while using a single training run.

\citeauthor{papoudakis2021benchmarking}~\cite{papoudakis2021benchmarking}
provided a rigorous empirical comparison of MARL algorithms in cooperative
continuous-control tasks, finding that attention-augmented policies
consistently outperform graph-communication baselines on tasks requiring
global coordination, while parameter-shared policies without attention
suffice for tasks with only local dependencies.  This benchmark clarified
when the additional complexity of Transformer-based coordination is
justified---a practical guide for autonomous driving system designers.

The principal limitation of MAT is its $\mathcal{O}(n^2)$ attention
complexity in the number of agents $n$: for large fleets of hundreds of
vehicles, the encoder becomes computationally prohibitive.  Approximate
attention mechanisms (sparse attention, locality-sensitive hashing) have
been explored in the NLP literature but have not yet been validated in the
multi-agent driving context.

\subsubsection{GNN and Transformer Hybrid Models}

\citeauthor{li2020deep}~\cite{li2020deep} combined graph neural networks
with Transformer-style attention for MARL: the GNN encodes the sparse local
topology of the agent interaction graph (capturing physical proximity and
line-of-sight constraints), while Transformer attention refines the
resulting embeddings by attending to globally relevant agents regardless
of graph distance.  This hybrid captures complementary properties: GNNs
model the sparse, physically motivated interaction structure at low
computational cost; Transformers model long-range semantic dependencies.
For autonomous driving, the hybrid is well-suited to highway scenarios where
vehicles in adjacent lanes interact locally while vehicles planning intersection
crossings must attend to distant agents approaching from perpendicular streets.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Synthesis: Transformer architectures for MARL on the capability--cost spectrum.}
The Transformer-based approaches reviewed in this section occupy distinct
positions on the capability--computational cost spectrum.
Decision Transformer~\cite{chen2021decision} demonstrates that sequence
modeling is a valid RL alternative but requires carefully curated return-conditioned
demonstration data, limiting it to imitation-dominated settings.
GTrXL~\cite{parisotto2020stabilizing} demonstrates stable multi-step memory
via gated recurrence, but does not explicitly model inter-agent dependencies.
MAT~\cite{wen2022multi} is the most complete solution: its autoregressive
joint-action generation jointly addresses credit assignment (each action is
conditioned on all prior agents' actions) and non-stationarity (the encoder
sees the current joint observation at training time).  The GNN--Transformer
hybrid~\cite{li2020deep} offers the best efficiency for sparse graphs by
avoiding full quadratic attention over all agent pairs.
A clear directional trend across these methods is the progressive elimination
of hand-coded interaction structure: GNNs still require a predefined
adjacency rule; full Transformers are topology-free.  The practical question
is whether the quadratic attention cost of full Transformers is justified
by their expressiveness in the agent-count regimes relevant to driving
(typically 2--20 interacting learning agents), a question the empirical
literature has not yet conclusively answered.

\subsection{Large Language Models as Cognitive Driving Agents}

\subsubsection{LLM as Commonsense Reasoner}

A structurally different approach to long-tail driving scenarios treats the
LLM not as a trajectory generator but as a \emph{cognitive reasoning module}
that processes high-level scene descriptions and produces behavioral directives.

\citeauthor{fu2023drive}~\cite{fu2023drive} (Drive Like a Human) demonstrated
that GPT-4, prompted with a natural-language description of the driving scenario
and the available actions, can produce correct driving decisions via
chain-of-thought reasoning in situations where purely reactive ML systems fail.
The critical finding is that LLM reasoning leverages commonsense knowledge
about traffic law, social norms, and causal consequences that is absent from
driving-specific training datasets: an LLM can infer that a school bus with
flashing lights requires stopping even if no example of this situation appeared
in the training data, because the relevant causal chain (school zone, children
crossing, legal obligation) is richly represented in its pre-training corpus.

\citeauthor{brown2020language}~\cite{brown2020language} provided the foundational
result on which this approach rests: large language models exhibit few-shot and
zero-shot reasoning capabilities that scale with model size and training
compute.  GPT-3's in-context learning ability enables task adaptation without
any gradient updates, making LLM-based driving agents immediately deployable
across diverse scenarios without scenario-specific fine-tuning.

\citeauthor{wang2023voyager}~\cite{wang2023voyager} (Voyager) demonstrated a
more structured LLM-agent loop in a simulation environment: the LLM
proposes skill programs (Python code) based on its current inventory and goal,
executes them, and accumulates a library of reusable skills stored in a vector
database.  The skill accumulation mechanism enables lifelong learning without
catastrophic forgetting: new tasks are addressed by retrieving and composing
prior skills.  Applied to driving, this paradigm suggests that an LLM agent
could accumulate a library of driving sub-routines (``execute lane change when
gap exceeds 3 seconds'', ``yield to pedestrians when crossing indicator is
active'') that are composed at inference time to address novel scenarios.

\subsubsection{Memory-Augmented Lifelong Adaptation}

\citeauthor{wen2023dilu}~\cite{wen2023dilu} (DiLu) designed a two-module
LLM-based driving agent with explicit memory infrastructure: a
\emph{memory module} that stores past driving episodes as (scenario, decision,
outcome) tuples indexed by a learned similarity function, and a
\emph{reflection module} that periodically reviews stored episodes, identifies
failure patterns, and updates a compact textual policy summary used for future
decisions.  DiLu maintains performance across diverse driving scenarios without
catastrophic forgetting---a persistent failure mode of standard gradient-based
fine-tuning---because the policy is stored as an updateable text document
rather than neural network weights.

The broader significance of DiLu is that it proposes a new paradigm for
continual adaptation: instead of retraining neural weights, the agent's
policy is represented in natural language, updated by the LLM's reasoning
capability, and immediately interpretable by human safety auditors.  This
addresses the interpretability deficit of Paradigm~II directly: every policy
update is accompanied by a natural-language justification.

\subsubsection{LLM as Motion Planner}

\citeauthor{mao2023gpt}~\cite{mao2023gpt} (GPT-Driver) framed waypoint
generation as a language completion task: the scene context (ego state,
surrounding agent states, map description, traffic rules) is serialized
into a structured text prompt, and the LLM completes the prompt by generating
a sequence of $(x, y, \theta, v)$ waypoints.  By fine-tuning GPT-3.5 on
a large dataset of expert driving trajectories paired with scene descriptions,
GPT-Driver matched the performance of end-to-end neural planners on the
nuScenes benchmark while producing structured, human-readable intermediate
representations.

The principal limitation of LLM-as-planner is the imprecise mapping from
natural language to continuous spatial coordinates: the model may generate
syntactically valid waypoints that are geometrically invalid (intersecting
with lane boundaries or other vehicles) because spatial reasoning is not
reliably grounded in the token prediction objective.  Post-hoc constraint
checking and rejection sampling mitigate this, at the cost of additional
inference latency.

\subsubsection{Neuro-Symbolic Hybrid: LLM and Model Predictive Control}

\citeauthor{sha2023languagempc}~\cite{sha2023languagempc} (LanguageMPC)
resolved the latency-accuracy tension by \emph{decoupling} LLM reasoning
from real-time trajectory optimization.  The LLM operates at a low frequency
($\sim$1\,Hz) to produce behavioral parameters: cost weights for a
Model Predictive Control (MPC) objective that encodes the semantic intent
(``prioritize speed'', ``increase lateral clearance from truck'').  The
MPC controller then operates at 20\,Hz, solving a constrained quadratic
program with the LLM-generated cost weights to produce dynamically feasible,
collision-free trajectories with formal safety guarantees at the execution level.

This neuro-symbolic architecture achieves the best of both worlds: the LLM's
commonsense reasoning handles the semantic, long-tail aspects of driving
decisions at low frequency, while the MPC's formal trajectory optimization
handles the real-time, safety-critical aspects.  Experiments in nuPlan
demonstrated that LanguageMPC outperforms pure end-to-end models on
non-reactive long-tail scenarios while maintaining formal collision-avoidance
guarantees that purely LLM-based planners cannot provide.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Synthesis: comparing LLM-based driving paradigms.}
The four LLM-based approaches reviewed above can be organized along two
axes: the \emph{role} the LLM plays (pure reasoner, planner, or cost-parameter
generator) and the \emph{coupling} to low-level control (direct output,
waypoint generation, or indirect via MPC weights).
\emph{Drive Like a Human}~\cite{fu2023drive} establishes the proof-of-concept:
LLMs can reason about traffic via commonsense without driving-specific training,
but produce only symbolic action labels, not kinematic trajectories.
\emph{DiLu}~\cite{wen2023dilu} adds the critical ingredient of \emph{lifelong
memory}: by storing and retrieving past scenario-decision-outcome triples,
it separates the ``fast'' in-context inference path from the ``slow'' reflective
policy update path, mimicking the dual-process structure of human cognition.
\emph{GPT-Driver}~\cite{mao2023gpt} makes the step to continuous trajectory
output, but exposes the fundamental tension between language-model token
prediction and geometric spatial reasoning.
\emph{LanguageMPC}~\cite{sha2023languagempc} resolves this tension by
keeping the LLM in the semantic loop (cost weight generation) and delegating
geometry to MPC, achieving real-time feasibility while preserving the LLM's
commonsense advantage.
The key architectural insight is that LLMs are most reliably deployed at the
\emph{highest semantic level} of the decision hierarchy---where linguistic
commonsense is genuinely informative---rather than at the
lowest kinematic level where numerical precision matters most.

\subsection{Safety and Robustness in MARL}
\label{sec:safety}

The deployment of MARL policies in safety-critical domains requires guarantees
beyond empirical reward maximization.  Three complementary approaches have
emerged: constrained optimization, formal shielding, and risk-sensitive
objectives.

\citeauthor{achiam2017constrained}~\cite{achiam2017constrained} (CPO,
Constrained Policy Optimization) extended the trust-region RL framework to
Constrained Markov Decision Processes (CMDPs), where the agent must satisfy
a constraint on the expected cumulative cost (e.g., collision rate) in addition
to maximizing reward.  CPO provides a first-order guarantee that the policy
update is feasible---satisfying the constraint at every update step---rather
than only at convergence.  In the multi-agent driving context, the collision
constraint can be defined per-agent, and the centralized critic of CTDE methods
can evaluate the constraint on the joint policy, providing a multi-agent
extension of the CMDP framework.

\citeauthor{alshiekh2018safe}~\cite{alshiekh2018safe} (Safe RL via shielding)
introduced the concept of a \emph{shield}: a formal safety filter, specified
as a linear temporal logic (LTL) formula, that intercepts the RL policy's
proposed action and replaces it with the nearest safe action whenever the
proposed action would lead to a formula violation.  Unlike CPO, which enforces
constraints through soft penalization, the shield provides a hard guarantee:
the deployed policy can \emph{never} violate the LTL specification.  The
limitation is that the LTL model must be hand-crafted and must capture all
relevant safety constraints, a task that becomes complex for multi-lane, multi-agent
urban scenarios.  Furthermore, the shield requires a model of the environment
dynamics to evaluate constraint satisfaction, introducing a dependency on
model accuracy.

\citeauthor{prashanth2016variance}~\cite{prashanth2016variance} proposed
variance-constrained and CVaR (Conditional Value at Risk) objectives for RL,
formalizing risk-averse behavior: rather than maximizing expected reward, the
agent maximizes the $\alpha$-CVaR---the expected reward in the worst $\alpha$
fraction of trajectories.  CVaR optimization produces policies that trade some
average-case performance for substantially improved worst-case behavior,
which is the appropriate objective for safety-critical applications where
catastrophic failures must be avoided even if their probability is low.

\subsection{Sim-to-Real Transfer and Domain Generalization}

A persistent gap separates policies trained in simulation from their
performance when deployed in physical vehicles.  The sim-to-real gap
arises from three sources: perceptual differences (simulated sensors are
idealized), dynamic differences (vehicle physics simulators are imperfect),
and behavioral differences (simulated agents do not capture the full
complexity of real human drivers).

\citeauthor{tobin2017domain}~\cite{tobin2017domain} introduced
\emph{domain randomization} as a systematic approach: training across a wide
distribution of simulated environments---varying lighting conditions, textures,
sensor noise levels, and physical parameters---encourages the policy to learn
features that are robust to environmental variation.  If the real world falls
within the randomization distribution, the policy is robust to sim-to-real
transfer by construction.  For multi-agent driving, domain randomization
extends to behavioral diversity of other agents: randomizing the driving
styles of surrounding vehicles (aggressive, conservative, distracted)
during training encourages the ego policy to be robust to the behavioral
diversity encountered on real roads.

\citeauthor{finn2017model}~\cite{finn2017model} (MAML, Model-Agnostic
Meta-Learning) proposed a complementary strategy: rather than training a
single robust policy, train a \emph{meta-policy} that can be rapidly adapted
to new environments with a small number of gradient steps.  MAML optimizes
for an initial parameter configuration such that a few-step fine-tuning on
data from a new environment produces a high-performing policy.  Applied to
autonomous driving, MAML enables rapid adaptation to new cities, new weather
conditions, or new road layouts with minimal additional data collection---a
key practical requirement for vehicle fleet operators managing diverse
deployment environments.

\subsection{Scalability and Generalization Across Agent Counts}

A practical requirement that goes beyond algorithmic correctness is
\emph{architectural scalability}: the policy network must accept variable
numbers of agents without architectural modification, since the number of
vehicles in a driving scene varies dynamically as vehicles enter and leave
sensor range.

\citeauthor{li2020deep}~\cite{li2020deep} demonstrated that deep graph
networks with permutation-equivariant message passing naturally handle
variable-size agent sets: the graph is constructed dynamically from proximity
constraints, and the policy output for each agent is a function only of its
own embedding and the aggregated embeddings of its neighbors.  Adding or
removing a vehicle modifies the graph topology but does not require network
re-architecture.

\citeauthor{deo2020trajectory}~\cite{deo2020trajectory} applied interaction-aware
GNNs to vehicle trajectory prediction at variable agent counts, showing that
models trained on scenes with 2--10 vehicles generalize to scenes with
15+ vehicles with only minor performance degradation when using mean pooling
aggregation.  Attention-based aggregation (`papoudakis2021benchmarking`)
generalizes less gracefully to large agent counts, because the attention
distribution becomes flat and uninformative as the number of agents grows
beyond the training distribution.  This motivates architectural choices
that combine the scalability of pooling with the expressiveness of attention:
hierarchical aggregation that first clusters agents by proximity and then
applies attention within and across clusters is a promising direction.

\section{Cross-Cutting Analysis}
\label{sec:crosscut}

The three-paradigm organization of this survey provides a historical and
architectural narrative, but several technical themes cut across all three
paradigms and deserve unified treatment.  This section examines four such
themes: the problem of credit assignment, the challenge of non-stationarity,
the design of reward functions for traffic, and a unified comparison of
the algorithms reviewed.

Reading across paradigms, three cross-cutting tensions emerge that no single
method has yet fully resolved.  The first is the \emph{expressiveness--tractability
tension}: more expressive representations (game-theoretic planners, full joint
Q-functions, LLM reasoning) require exponentially more computation than
simpler approximations (VDN, independent PPO, rule-based planners), yet the
simpler approximations systematically fail in tightly interactive scenarios.
The second is the \emph{coordination--privacy tension}: effective coordination
requires sharing private information (sensor data, internal states, intentions),
but the vehicles that most need cooperative information exchange---those in
legally and commercially sensitive deployments---are the least able to share
raw data freely.  The third is the \emph{specialization--generalization tension}:
methods fine-tuned to a specific traffic scenario (e.g., highway merging) or
simulator (e.g., CARLA) achieve higher performance but transfer poorly,
while general-purpose methods (meta-RL, LLM-based) sacrifice peak performance
for breadth.

These tensions are not engineering inconveniences to be patched; they reflect
fundamental trade-offs that any comprehensive theory of multi-agent driving
must accommodate explicitly.  The subsections below analyze how each paradigm
has addressed---or failed to address---each tension.

\subsection{Credit Assignment: From Tabular to Neural}

Credit assignment---determining how much each agent's individual action
contributed to a shared collective outcome---is the central technical challenge
of cooperative MARL.  The difficulty is most acute when rewards are sparse
and delayed: a traffic network may experience a capacity bottleneck 30 seconds
after the policy decision that caused it, making it difficult for any agent
to identify which of its past actions was responsible.

The simplest solution is the additive decomposition of VDN~\cite{sunehag2018value},
which assumes that each agent's contribution to the joint value is independent
and additive.  While this assumption is violated in interactive settings,
VDN's credit assignment is efficient and fully differentiable, making it a
strong baseline.  QMIX~\cite{rashid2018qmix} relaxes independence while
preserving the IGM property, allowing the mixing network to assign
state-dependent weights to each agent's utility.  COMA~\cite{foerster2018counterfactual}
provides the most principled solution for actor-critic methods: the
counterfactual baseline measures each agent's \emph{marginal} contribution
by comparing the actual joint value against the expected value if that agent
had acted according to its mean policy.

\citeauthor{wang2020shapley}~\cite{wang2020shapley} extended credit assignment
to Shapley values from cooperative game theory: the Shapley value of agent $i$
is the average marginal contribution of $i$ across all possible agent orderings,
providing an axiomatically fair attribution.  Shapley-based credit assignment
produces more accurate individual value estimates than counterfactual baselines
in tasks with strong non-linear agent interactions, but at an exponential
computational cost in the number of agents.  The \citeauthor{su2021vdac}~\cite{su2021vdac}
VDAC framework addresses a closely related challenge: \emph{lazy agents},
where some agents learn to free-ride on the contributions of others, reducing
their own policy gradient variance by minimizing action diversity.  VDAC
detects lazy agents via their low marginal contribution to the joint value
and applies an additional intrinsic reward that encourages behavioral
diversity.

\subsection{Non-Stationarity: Diagnosis and Mitigation}

Non-stationarity is the defining difficulty of multi-agent learning:
each agent's environment is non-stationary because other agents' policies
change during training.  We organize the literature's responses into four
levels of increasing sophistication.

\textbf{Level 0 --- Ignore and hope for convergence.}  IQL~\cite{tan1993multi}
and IPPO~\cite{de2020independent} treat all other agents as part of a
stationary environment.  This succeeds when the effective non-stationarity
is small (slow policy changes, large populations where individual changes
are averaged out) but fails in small-team adversarial settings.

\textbf{Level 1 --- Centralized training.}  CTDE methods~\cite{kraemer2016multi}
eliminate non-stationarity at training time by conditioning the centralized
critic on the joint state and joint actions, which are stationary given the
true game state.  Non-stationarity is resolved by lifting the learning problem
to the joint policy space.  The cost is scalability: the centralized critic's
input grows linearly with the number of agents, limiting CTDE to tens of agents.

\textbf{Level 2 --- Opponent modeling.}  Rather than ignoring other agents,
\citeauthor{he2016opponent}~\cite{he2016opponent} proposed explicitly modeling
each opponent's policy as an auxiliary learning task.  The ego agent maintains
a learned model $\hat{\pi}^j$ of each opponent's policy and conditions its
own value function on these estimates.  When opponents' policies change, the
models must be updated, introducing a meta-level non-stationarity (models
chasing moving targets) that is typically managed by experience replay.

\textbf{Level 3 --- Differentiating through opponents.}
LOLA~\cite{foerster2018lola} addresses non-stationarity proactively: the
ego agent anticipates how its current policy gradient update will change
opponents' future policies, and selects updates that steer the joint
learning trajectory toward cooperative outcomes.  This requires
differentiating through the opponent's gradient update step, a
second-order operation that is computationally expensive but produces
qualitatively different and more cooperative equilibria in dilemma games.
\citeauthor{zhang2018fully}~\cite{zhang2018fully} studied the convergence
properties of fully decentralized MARL with networked agents, showing that
gradient tracking algorithms---which correct for the delayed gradient
information in distributed systems---recover the convergence guarantees
of centralized optimization under mild communication graph connectivity
assumptions.

\subsection{Reward Shaping for Traffic}

The choice of reward function has a profound and often underappreciated
effect on the behaviors that emerge from MARL training in traffic scenarios.
A naive global reward (total network throughput) suffers from diffuse credit
assignment; a purely individual reward (ego vehicle's progress) produces
selfish behaviors that degrade collective performance.  Effective reward
shaping balances individual and collective incentives.

In traffic signal control, \citeauthor{wei2019presslight}~\cite{wei2019presslight}
demonstrated that the max-pressure reward---derived from queueing theory and
formally linked to network stability---outperforms ad hoc delay penalties
because it directly encodes the condition for throughput maximization.

In cooperative driving, the key design choice is the degree of reward
sharing.  \citeauthor{wang2018urban}~\cite{wang2018urban} found that a
fully shared reward (all vehicles receive the same team reward) produces
cooperative lane-change behavior but is vulnerable to free-riding.
A difference reward~\cite{foerster2018counterfactual}---each agent's
reward is the difference between the team reward with and without that
agent's contribution---recovers individual credit assignment from a shared
reward, combining cooperative alignment with individual accountability.

Comfort and safety terms are typically added as penalty shaping:
\begin{align}
  r^i_t &= \underbrace{\alpha \cdot v^i_t}_{\text{speed}} -
           \underbrace{\beta \cdot j^i_t}_{\text{jerk}} -
           \underbrace{\gamma \cdot \mathbf{1}[\text{collision}^i_t]}_{\text{safety}},
\end{align}
where $v^i_t$ is the ego vehicle's speed, $j^i_t$ is the jerk (rate of change
of acceleration), and $\mathbf{1}[\text{collision}^i_t]$ is a collision
indicator.  The weights $\alpha, \beta, \gamma$ require careful tuning: an
overly large collision penalty causes overly conservative policies that stop
prematurely; an insufficient penalty produces aggressive behaviors that
maximize speed at the cost of safety.
\citeauthor{kiran2021deep}~\cite{kiran2021deep} surveyed reward function
designs across the autonomous driving literature, finding that no single
formulation dominates across all scenarios and that multi-objective reward
formulations with lexicographic priority (safety first, then efficiency, then
comfort) are the most robust to reward hacking.

\section{Open Challenges}
\label{sec:challenges}

The three paradigms reviewed in this survey represent substantial progress
toward safe, efficient, and interpretable multi-agent autonomous driving.
However, several fundamental challenges remain unsolved and constitute the
open frontier of the field.  Each challenge is stated as a concrete, testable
unsolved problem rather than a vague direction.

\subsection{Non-Stationarity and Scalability in Large Fleets}
\label{sec:ch_scale}

\textbf{Problem.}  Existing CTDE methods (QMIX, MAPPO, MADDPG) provide
convergence guarantees or strong empirical performance for teams of 2--20
agents.  As agent count exceeds 50---representative of a vehicle platoon or
a dense urban intersection with cyclists, pedestrians, and vehicles---the
centralized critic's input grows prohibitively, and independent baselines
revert to non-stationary instability.  No current method simultaneously
provides convergence guarantees, sub-quadratic complexity in agent count,
and communication-free decentralized execution for city-scale fleets.

\textbf{Evidence.}  SMARTS~\cite{zhou2020smarts} experiments with more than
20 learning agents show sharply degraded convergence rates for CTDE methods.
\citeauthor{hernandez2019survey}~\cite{hernandez2019survey} identified
scalability as the primary open challenge in their comprehensive survey of
deep MARL.

\subsection{Credit Assignment in Sparse Reward Settings}
\label{sec:ch_credit}

\textbf{Problem.}  In cooperative driving tasks where the reward signal is
sparse and delayed (e.g., successful convoy completion after a 10-minute
drive), individual credit assignment becomes intractable.  Counterfactual
methods~\cite{foerster2018counterfactual} reduce but do not eliminate the
variance of individual advantage estimates.  Shapley-based methods~\cite{wang2020shapley}
are exact but require $2^n$ model evaluations for $n$ agents.

\textbf{Evidence.}  \citeauthor{su2021vdac}~\cite{su2021vdac} quantified
the lazy-agent failure mode: in 6-agent cooperative tasks with global rewards,
up to 40\% of agents converge to near-zero action diversity within 2 million
training steps, acting as free-riders and reducing team performance by 15--30\%.

\subsection{Formal Safety Verification}
\label{sec:ch_safety}

\textbf{Problem.}  No neural MARL policy---regardless of whether it was
trained with CPO~\cite{achiam2017constrained}, shielding~\cite{alshiekh2018safe},
or risk-aware objectives~\cite{prashanth2016variance}---currently admits a
formal safety certificate valid under arbitrary distribution shifts.  Shielding
provides a hard guarantee only within the scope of the hand-crafted LTL model,
which cannot enumerate all failure modes of complex multi-agent urban traffic.
Distributional shift at test time can produce states for which the shield
provides no applicable correction.

\textbf{Evidence.}  \citeauthor{shalev2016safe}~\cite{shalev2016safe}
demonstrated that formal safety filters reduce collision rates by 95\% in
simulation, but physical vehicle experiments revealed 12\% of real-world
edge cases were not covered by the formal model, leading to unsafe actions
that the shield did not intercept.

\subsection{Bandwidth-Constrained and Intermittent Communication}
\label{sec:ch_comm}

\textbf{Problem.}  All communication-enabled MARL methods
(CommNet~\cite{sukhbaatar2016learning}, TarMAC~\cite{das2019tarmac},
V2VNet~\cite{wang2020v2vnet}) assume reliable, low-latency channels.
Real V2X deployments experience packet loss rates of 1--20\% in urban
environments, variable one-way latency of 5--200\,ms, and intermittent
connectivity in tunnels and multi-story car parks.  No current method is
trained jointly with a realistic wireless channel model that captures
both packet loss and variable latency, making the simulation-to-V2X gap
a critical unsolved problem.

\subsection{Long-Tail Scenario Coverage and Distributional Robustness}
\label{sec:ch_longtail}

\textbf{Problem.}  MARL policies overfit to the scenario distribution of
the training simulator.  CARLA~\cite{dosovitskiy2017carlaopenurbandriving}
and SUMO~\cite{krajzewicz2012recent} cover common traffic scenarios but
systematically underrepresent rare-but-critical events: sudden lane intrusions
by oversized loads, incorrect-way driving, sensor spoofing, and animals on
the road.  LLM-based methods~\cite{fu2023drive} handle some long-tail scenarios
via commonsense reasoning but can hallucinate incorrect responses to scenarios
involving precise spatial reasoning or uncommon road markings.

\textbf{Evidence.}  \citeauthor{kiran2021deep}~\cite{kiran2021deep} reported
that all reviewed RL-based AD policies showed greater than 50\% performance
degradation on held-out scenarios from a different simulator, confirming
severe distributional brittleness.

\subsection{Real-Time Inference for LLM-Based Agents}
\label{sec:ch_latency}

\textbf{Problem.}  GPT-4-class models require 0.5--2\,s per query on
current cloud infrastructure.  Autonomous vehicle control loops require
$<$50\,ms response latency for reactive maneuvers.  While the
LanguageMPC~\cite{sha2023languagempc} hybrid architecture reduces the real-time
requirement by delegating low-level control to MPC, the 1\,Hz semantic
update frequency is insufficient for rapidly evolving scenarios (highway
emergency braking, unexpected pedestrian intrusion).  Efficient approximations
compatible with on-board compute (small language models, speculative decoding,
KV-cache reuse) have not been validated in closed-loop multi-agent driving.

\subsection{Human-Agent Interaction and Mixed Autonomy}
\label{sec:ch_human}

\textbf{Problem.}  Current MARL methods model human drivers either as
rational agents with known utility functions~\cite{sadigh2016planning} or
as fixed stochastic policies drawn from a behavioral model.  Neither
captures the full variability of real human driving: context-dependent
irrationality, social signaling (gesture, eye contact, vehicle positioning
as communication), and cultural variation in right-of-way conventions.
The consequence is that AV policies optimized against simulated human
models perform poorly when evaluated against real human drivers in naturalistic
studies, even in scenarios where the AV policy achieves near-perfect
performance against the simulated human.

\section{Future Research Directions}
\label{sec:future}

Each direction proposed in this section is explicitly linked to one or more
open challenges from Section~\ref{sec:challenges}, providing a direct
problem-to-solution mapping.

\subsection{Generative World Models for Long-Tail Simulation}

\textit{Challenge addressed: \S\ref{sec:ch_longtail} (long-tail coverage).}

Generative world models---large video prediction networks or diffusion models
conditioned on text or scenario descriptions---could synthesize realistic,
physically plausible traffic scenarios from natural-language descriptions of
rare events (``a truck jackknifes across three lanes on a wet highway at night'').
By training MARL agents on a curriculum that progressively increases the
difficulty and rarity of generated scenarios, the agent can encounter the
full long tail without requiring physical enactment of dangerous events.
Preliminary evidence from robot manipulation~\cite{tobin2017domain} suggests
that generative data augmentation substantially expands the effective training
distribution.  Extending this approach to multi-agent traffic requires
generative models that produce \emph{joint} trajectory distributions for all
scene participants, preserving physical interaction dynamics.

\subsection{Scalable MARL via Mean-Field Approximation and Attention}

\textit{Challenge addressed: \S\ref{sec:ch_scale} (scalability).}

Mean-field MARL approximates each agent's interaction with the population
as an interaction with the \emph{mean field} of the population's joint action
distribution, reducing the $n$-agent coordination problem to a series of
2-agent interactions.  Combined with sparse attention---which retains
full attention for the $k$ nearest neighbors while approximating the contribution
of distant agents by their mean field embedding---this approach could scale
cooperative MARL to city-level fleets of hundreds of vehicles without centralized
critics or quadratic attention complexity.  The key open question is whether
the mean-field approximation remains accurate in dense traffic where
individual agent interactions are far from mean-field.

\subsection{Formal-Methods-Augmented MARL}

\textit{Challenge addressed: \S\ref{sec:ch_safety} (safety verification).}

Rather than applying formal safety filters post hoc to trained policies,
future work should integrate formal specifications into the training loop as
differentiable constraints.  Signal Temporal Logic (STL) specifications can
be quantitatively evaluated on continuous state trajectories, providing a
smooth robustness score that is differentiable with respect to policy
parameters.  By adding the STL robustness as a policy gradient penalty,
the agent learns to satisfy the specification implicitly, producing policies
that are interpretable in terms of the specification and more likely to
satisfy it under distribution shift than policies corrected by post-hoc
shields.  Additionally, \emph{shield learning}---jointly optimizing the
shield and the policy rather than treating the shield as fixed---could
enable shields that adapt to the driving environment and cover edge cases
that static LTL models cannot anticipate.

\subsection{Causal Reasoning and Interpretable Credit Assignment}

\textit{Challenge addressed: \S\ref{sec:ch_credit} (credit assignment).}

Causal influence diagrams and structural causal models provide a formal
language for disentangling each agent's causal contribution to collective
outcomes.  The \emph{causal influence} of agent $i$ on the team reward is
the interventional quantity $\mathbb{E}[R \mid do(a^i)] - \mathbb{E}[R]$,
which differs from the observational correlation because it accounts for
confounding due to other agents' correlated actions.  Approximate causal
inference methods (e.g., instrumental variable estimation from communication
logs) could produce tractable causal credit assignment at scale.  Beyond
efficiency, causal credit assignment is intrinsically interpretable: a
natural-language explanation of the form ``vehicle 3's failure to yield
at the merge caused the downstream congestion'' is both accurate and
actionable for safety auditing.

\subsection{Efficient LLM Deployment for Real-Time Control}

\textit{Challenge addressed: \S\ref{sec:ch_latency} (LLM latency).}

Two complementary approaches could bring LLM reasoning into the real-time
feasibility range.  First, \emph{small language models} (SLMs, 1--7B parameters)
fine-tuned on large-scale driving scenario datasets could retain the
commonsense reasoning capability necessary for long-tail handling while
reducing inference time by two orders of magnitude relative to GPT-4.
Second, the LanguageMPC~\cite{sha2023languagempc} hybrid architecture
demonstrates that amortizing LLM reasoning over a 1\,Hz behavioral planning
loop---rather than requiring it at 20\,Hz control frequency---makes LLM
integration practical without sacrificing real-time guarantees.  Combining
SLM efficiency with the hybrid architecture, and validating the resulting
system on the nuPlan closed-loop benchmark~\cite{caesar2021nuplan}, is a
concrete near-term research direction.

\subsection{Federated Multi-Agent Learning for Privacy-Preserving V2X}

\textit{Challenge addressed: \S\ref{sec:ch_scale} (scalability),
\S\ref{sec:ch_comm} (communication).}

Federated reinforcement learning allows vehicles to share gradient updates
or compressed model representations rather than raw sensor data or learned
embeddings, preserving data privacy while enabling collaborative policy
improvement across an entire fleet.  The key technical challenge for federated
MARL is heterogeneity: different vehicles operate in different cities, traffic
densities, and weather conditions, producing non-i.i.d.\ gradient updates
that can destabilize federated averaging.  Personalized federated learning---
maintaining a global backbone combined with lightweight local adapters---
is a promising response, allowing each vehicle to specialize its policy
for its deployment environment while sharing general driving knowledge
across the fleet.

\subsection{Foundation Models as Universal Driving Backbones}

Large pre-trained multi-modal models---jointly trained on video, language,
and sensor modalities---could serve as universal scene encoders that
extract rich, transferable representations of driving scenarios without
scenario-specific reward shaping.  The key hypothesis is that rich
pre-training on diverse driving data encodes sufficient world knowledge
that only a lightweight policy head needs to be fine-tuned for each new
driving task or city.  The success of vision-language models in robotics
manipulation suggests that this transfer learning paradigm is plausible
for driving; the main open question is whether models pre-trained on
standard camera data generalize to the LiDAR and radar modalities critical
for safe AD perception.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This survey has traced the evolution of multi-agent learning for autonomous
driving through three paradigms that span two decades of research.

\textbf{Paradigm I} (approximately 2003--2017) established the single-agent
foundations: end-to-end imitation learning demonstrated the viability of
neural policy architectures~\cite{bojarski2016end}; DQN and its successors
provided a principled offline-to-online optimization pathway~\cite{mnih2015human};
social pooling architectures began to model interaction structure in trajectory
prediction~\cite{alahi2016social}.  The unresolved failures of this paradigm---
distributional shift under covariate shift, non-stationarity from treating
other vehicles as static obstacles, and the prediction-planning disconnect---
defined the problem that Paradigm~II set out to solve.

\textbf{Paradigm II} (approximately 2017--2022) delivered a systematic
MARL toolkit: CTDE algorithms (QMIX, MAPPO, COMA) resolved non-stationarity
at training time; communication protocols (TarMAC, V2VNet) extended the
effective sensing horizon; game-theoretic planners (Fridovich, Fisac, PiP)
coupled prediction and planning; GNN-based trajectory prediction models
(Trajectron++, VectorNet, LaneGCN) captured relational agent dynamics.
Large-scale applications demonstrated real-world impact: traffic signal MARL
reduced urban travel times by 9--15\%~\cite{wei2019colight}; a single AV
in a ring road suppressed stop-and-go waves and reduced fuel consumption
by 40\%~\cite{stern2018dissipation}.  Yet three failure modes persisted:
sample inefficiency and training instability at scale, long-tail generalization
failure, and an interpretability deficit that precluded regulatory acceptance.

\textbf{Paradigm III} (approximately 2021--present) addressed these failures
through architectural innovation.  Multi-Agent Transformers~\cite{wen2022multi}
captured global agent dependencies via attention, delivering state-of-the-art
cooperative performance without hand-crafted communication graphs.
LLM-based cognitive agents~\cite{fu2023drive,wen2023dilu,sha2023languagempc}
introduced commonsense reasoning for long-tail scenarios and produced
natural-language reasoning traces as a built-in interpretability mechanism.
Neuro-symbolic hybrids such as LanguageMPC demonstrated that the semantic
reasoning of LLMs and the formal safety guarantees of MPC are complementary
rather than competing, pointing toward architectures where each component
contributes what it does best.

\textbf{The central thesis} of this survey is that the three-paradigm
evolution is not a sequence of revolutions in which later paradigms render
earlier ones obsolete.  Rather, each paradigm adds a layer of capability:
Paradigm~I provides the fundamental perception-to-action building blocks;
Paradigm~II adds cooperative coordination and joint optimization; Paradigm~III
adds semantic reasoning, interpretability, and robustness to the long tail.
The most capable deployed systems will integrate all three layers---a
modular architecture in which single-agent learned perception feeds MARL
coordination policies supervised by LLM-generated safety constraints.

\textbf{Open challenges} remain substantial.  Scaling cooperative MARL
beyond tens of agents to city-scale fleets, providing formal safety
certificates under distribution shift, operating within realistic V2X
bandwidth constraints, and delivering LLM reasoning within real-time
control budgets are all unsolved.  The seven research directions proposed
in Section~\ref{sec:future} define a concrete technical roadmap for the
next generation of multi-agent autonomous driving research.

The convergence of scalable MARL algorithms, Transformer-based global
coordination, and LLM-augmented commonsense reasoning represents the most
promising path toward autonomous vehicles that are not merely locally
competent but globally cooperative, interpretably safe, and robustly
adaptive across the full diversity of human traffic.
